Magnetic Materials, Inductance, and Energy

Magnetic materials determine how strongly a current produces magnetic flux and how much energy is stored or lost in a magnetic system. In free space, B⃗=μ0H⃗\vec B=\mu_0\vec HB=μ0​H. In matter, electron orbital and spin moments contribute magnetization, so the relationship can be linear, nonlinear, hysteretic, or anisotropic. Engineering devices such as solenoids, transformers, inductive sensors, motors, and magnetic storage depend on these material effects.

Inductance is the circuit-level measure of magnetic flux linkage per current. Like capacitance, it is fundamentally geometric and material. A current creates H⃗\vec HH, the medium produces B⃗\vec BB, flux links conductors, and energy is stored in the magnetic field. This page covers magnetic materials, boundary conditions, self and mutual inductance, and magnetic energy.

Definitions​

Magnetization M⃗\vec MM is magnetic dipole moment per unit volume. The macroscopic fields satisfy

B⃗=μ0(H⃗+M⃗).\vec B=\mu_0(\vec H+\vec M).B=μ0​(H+M).

For a linear isotropic material,

M⃗=χmH⃗,B⃗=μH⃗=μ0μrH⃗,\vec M=\chi_m\vec H,\qquad \vec B=\mu\vec H=\mu_0\mu_r\vec H,M=χm​H,B=μH=μ0​μr​H,

where

μr=1+χm.\mu_r=1+\chi_m.μr​=1+χm​.

Materials are often grouped as diamagnetic (χm<0\chi_m\lt 0χm​<0 small), paramagnetic (χm>0\chi_m\gt 0χm​>0 small), and ferromagnetic (large, nonlinear, often hysteretic).

Ferromagnetic behavior is especially important in engineering because large permeability can concentrate flux and greatly increase inductance. The tradeoff is nonlinearity. As HHH increases, domains become increasingly aligned and the material approaches saturation; additional current then produces much less additional BBB. A design that assumes constant high μr\mu_rμr​ beyond saturation can underpredict current, overpredict inductance, and miss heating or waveform distortion.

Magnetic boundary conditions follow from ∇⋅B⃗=0\nabla\cdot\vec B=0∇⋅B=0 and Ampere's law. At an interface with unit normal n^\hat nn^ from medium 1 to medium 2,

n^⋅(B⃗2−B⃗1)=0,\hat n\cdot(\vec B_2-\vec B_1)=0,n^⋅(B2​−B1​)=0,

and

n^×(H⃗2−H⃗1)=J⃗s,\hat n\times(\vec H_2-\vec H_1)=\vec J_s,n^×(H2​−H1​)=Js​,

where J⃗s\vec J_sJs​ is free surface current density.

Flux linkage is

Λ=NΦ,\Lambda=N\Phi,Λ=NΦ,

and self-inductance is

L=ΛI.L=\frac{\Lambda}{I}.L=IΛ​.

Mutual inductance between circuits is

M21=Λ21I1,M_{21}=\frac{\Lambda_{21}}{I_1},M21​=I1​Λ21​​,

where Λ21\Lambda_{21}Λ21​ is flux linkage of circuit 2 caused by current I1I_1I1​.

Magnetic energy density in a linear medium is

wm=12B⃗⋅H⃗=12μH2.w_m=\frac{1}{2}\vec B\cdot\vec H=\frac{1}{2}\mu H^2.wm​=21​B⋅H=21​μH2.

Key results​

For a long solenoid with NNN turns, length lll, current III, and core permeability μ\muμ, Ampere's law gives approximately

H=NIlH=\frac{NI}{l}H=lNI​

inside the solenoid. If the cross-sectional area is AAA, the flux is

Φ=BA=μHA=μNIlA.\Phi=BA=\mu HA=\mu\frac{NI}{l}A.Φ=BA=μHA=μlNI​A.

Flux linkage is NΦN\PhiNΦ, so

L=NΦI=μN2Al.L=\frac{N\Phi}{I}=\frac{\mu N^2A}{l}.L=INΦ​=lμN2A​.

This formula is approximate because it neglects fringing and assumes uniform field, but it captures the scaling: inductance increases with permeability, turns squared, and area, and decreases with magnetic path length.

For a coaxial line with inner radius aaa, outer conductor inner radius bbb, and nonmagnetic dielectric, the external magnetic field between conductors is

Hϕ=I2πρ.H_\phi=\frac{I}{2\pi\rho}.Hϕ​=2πρI​.

The magnetic energy per unit length is

Wm′=∫ab12μHϕ2(2πρ dρ),W_m'=\int_a^b \frac{1}{2}\mu H_\phi^2(2\pi\rho\,d\rho),Wm′​=∫ab​21​μHϕ2​(2πρdρ),

and setting Wm′=12L′I2W_m'=\frac{1}{2}L'I^2Wm′​=21​L′I2 gives

L′=μ2πln⁡ba.L'=\frac{\mu}{2\pi}\ln\frac{b}{a}.L′=2πμ​lnab​.

Hysteresis in ferromagnetic materials means the BBB-HHH curve depends on history. Energy is lost as heat over a cycle, with loss per unit volume equal to the area enclosed by the hysteresis loop.

Magnetic circuits provide a useful approximation for core devices. Reluctance R=l/(μA)\mathcal R=l/(\mu A)R=l/(μA) plays a role analogous to resistance, flux Φ\PhiΦ is analogous to current, and magnetomotive force NININI is analogous to voltage. The analogy is not exact because fringing, leakage flux, saturation, and hysteresis are field effects, but it gives fast estimates for transformers, inductors, relays, and electromagnets.

Mutual inductance depends on how much flux from one circuit links another. The coupling coefficient kkk is defined by

M=kL1L2,0≤k≤1.M=k\sqrt{L_1L_2},\qquad 0\le k\le1.M=kL1​L2​​,0≤k≤1.

Ideal transformers assume tight coupling, negligible winding resistance, and negligible core loss. Real transformers depart from this through leakage inductance, winding capacitance, finite permeability, and frequency-dependent losses.

Magnetic energy is sometimes counterintuitive in high-permeability cores. Since wm=12BH=B22μw_m=\frac{1}{2}BH=\frac{B^2}{2\mu}wm​=21​BH=2μB2​ for a linear material, a high-μ\muμ region can carry large flux density with relatively small HHH and comparatively low energy density for a given BBB. Air gaps often store a significant fraction of the energy in inductors because the gap has low permeability and therefore requires larger HHH. This is why gapped cores are used when an inductor must store energy without saturating.

Boundary conditions explain flux concentration. Normal B⃗\vec BB is continuous across an interface, but tangential H⃗\vec HH changes according to permeability when there is no surface current. Flux tends to prefer high-permeability paths because they require less magnetomotive force for a given flux. The word "prefer" is only shorthand; the actual field still satisfies Maxwell's equations and material relations everywhere.

Core loss has multiple mechanisms. Hysteresis loss comes from cycling around a BBB-HHH loop. Eddy-current loss comes from currents induced inside conducting magnetic material by time-varying flux. Laminated steel, ferrites, powdered iron, and gapped cores are different engineering responses to these losses and saturation limits. The best core material depends strongly on frequency, flux density, waveform, temperature, and required energy storage.

Inductance measurements can also be frequency dependent. At low frequency, winding resistance and core permeability may dominate. At high frequency, skin effect, proximity effect, inter-winding capacitance, and core dispersion alter the apparent impedance. A quoted inductance value is therefore incomplete without test frequency and bias conditions for precision work.

The polarity of mutual inductance is tracked with dot convention in circuits. If currents enter dotted terminals simultaneously and their fluxes reinforce, the mutual voltage terms have the same sign. The dot notation is a compact circuit symbol for magnetic-field orientation and winding sense; assigning it carelessly can reverse transformer or coupled-inductor behavior.

Always pair inductance formulas with the assumed flux path.

Worked example 1: Inductance of a long solenoid​

Problem: A solenoid has N=600N=600N=600 turns, length l=0.30l=0.30l=0.30 m, cross-sectional area A=4.0×10−4 m2A=4.0\times10^{-4}\ \mathrm{m^2}A=4.0×10−4 m2, and a nonmagnetic core. Find LLL.

Step 1: Use the long-solenoid formula:

L=μN2Al.L=\frac{\mu N^2A}{l}.L=lμN2A​.

Step 2: For a nonmagnetic core, μ=μ0=4π×10−7 H/m\mu=\mu_0=4\pi\times10^{-7}\ \mathrm{H/m}μ=μ0​=4π×10−7 H/m.

Step 3: Substitute:

L=(4π×10−7)(600)2(4.0×10−4)0.30.L=\frac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(600)^2(4.0\times10^{-4})}{0.30}.L=0.30(4π×10−7)(600)2(4.0×10−4)​.

Step 4: Compute N2AN^2AN2A:

(600)2(4.0×10−4)=360000(4.0×10−4)=144.(600)^2(4.0\times10^{-4})=360000(4.0\times10^{-4})=144.(600)2(4.0×10−4)=360000(4.0×10−4)=144.

Step 5: Finish:

L=(4π×10−7)(144)0.30=6.03×10−4 H.L=\frac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(144)}{0.30} =6.03\times10^{-4}\ \mathrm{H}.L=0.30(4π×10−7)(144)​=6.03×10−4 H.

Answer:

L=0.603 mH.L=0.603\ \mathrm{mH}.L=0.603 mH.

Check: If a high-μr\mu_rμr​ core were inserted without saturation, LLL would scale upward by approximately μr\mu_rμr​.

Worked example 2: Coaxial inductance per unit length​

Problem: A coaxial line has a=0.5a=0.5a=0.5 mm and b=2.5b=2.5b=2.5 mm with nonmagnetic dielectric. Find L′L'L′ neglecting internal conductor inductance.

Step 1: Use the external inductance formula:

L′=μ2πln⁡ba.L'=\frac{\mu}{2\pi}\ln\frac{b}{a}.L′=2πμ​lnab​.

Step 2: Substitute μ=μ0\mu=\mu_0μ=μ0​ and b/a=5b/a=5b/a=5:

L′=4π×10−72πln⁡5.L'=\frac{4\pi\times10^{-7}}{2\pi}\ln 5.L′=2π4π×10−7​ln5.

Step 3: Simplify the coefficient:

4π×10−72π=2.0×10−7 H/m.\frac{4\pi\times10^{-7}}{2\pi}=2.0\times10^{-7}\ \mathrm{H/m}.2π4π×10−7​=2.0×10−7 H/m.

Step 4: Compute:

L′=(2.0×10−7)(1.609)=3.22×10−7 H/m.L'=(2.0\times10^{-7})(1.609)=3.22\times10^{-7}\ \mathrm{H/m}.L′=(2.0×10−7)(1.609)=3.22×10−7 H/m.

Answer:

L′=0.322 μH/m.L'=0.322\ \mu\mathrm{H/m}.L′=0.322 μH/m.

Check: Together with a typical coaxial capacitance of tens of pF/m, this gives a characteristic impedance in the tens of ohms, consistent with practical cables.

Code​

import numpy as np

mu0 = 4 * np.pi * 1e-7

def solenoid_L(N, area, length, mu_r=1):
    return mu0 * mu_r * N**2 * area / length

def coax_L_per_m(a, b, mu_r=1):
    return mu0 * mu_r / (2 * np.pi) * np.log(b / a)

print("solenoid L =", solenoid_L(600, 4e-4, 0.30), "H")
print("coax L' =", coax_L_per_m(0.5e-3, 2.5e-3), "H/m")

Common pitfalls​

  • Assuming μr\mu_rμr​ is constant for ferromagnetic materials. Saturation and hysteresis can dominate real designs.
  • Confusing flux Φ\PhiΦ with flux linkage Λ=NΦ\Lambda=N\PhiΛ=NΦ.
  • Forgetting the N2N^2N2 scaling of solenoid inductance.
  • Using electric boundary-condition intuition for magnetic fields. Normal B⃗\vec BB is continuous; tangential H⃗\vec HH jumps with surface current.
  • Ignoring fringing in short coils or magnetic gaps.
  • Equating high permeability with unlimited energy storage. High-μ\muμ cores can saturate and often introduce loss.
  • Forgetting that inductance can depend on current in nonlinear magnetic materials.
