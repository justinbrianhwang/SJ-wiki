Electrostatic Fields and Potential

Electrostatics studies electric fields produced by stationary charge distributions. It is a special case of Maxwell's equations in which fields do not vary with time and magnetic induction is absent from the electric-field equation. The central objects are charge density, electric field intensity E⃗\vec EE, electric flux density D⃗\vec DD, scalar potential VVV, and the force on charge.

The value of electrostatics is larger than "charges at rest." Capacitance, insulation design, electrostatic sensors, semiconductor depletion regions, and many boundary-value methods all begin here. Later, time-varying electromagnetics keeps the same electric field concepts but couples them to magnetic fields through Faraday's law and the displacement current.

Definitions​

Charge can be modeled in several idealized ways:

Q=∫Lρl dl,Q=∫Sρs dS,Q=∫Vρv dv,Q=\int_L \rho_l\,dl,\qquad Q=\int_S \rho_s\,dS,\qquad Q=\int_V \rho_v\,dv,Q=∫L​ρl​dl,Q=∫S​ρs​dS,Q=∫V​ρv​dv,

where ρl\rho_lρl​ is line charge density in C/m, ρs\rho_sρs​ is surface charge density in C/m2^22, and ρv\rho_vρv​ is volume charge density in C/m3^33.

Coulomb's law for the force on q2q_2q2​ due to q1q_1q1​ is

F⃗12=q1q24πϵR2R^,\vec F_{12}=\frac{q_1q_2}{4\pi\epsilon R^2}\hat R,F12​=4πϵR2q1​q2​​R^,

where R⃗=r⃗2−r⃗1\vec R=\vec r_2-\vec r_1R=r2​−r1​ points from q1q_1q1​ to q2q_2q2​. The electric field is force per unit test charge:

E⃗=F⃗q.\vec E=\frac{\vec F}{q}.E=qF​.

For a point charge qqq at the origin in a homogeneous medium,

E⃗=q4πϵr2r^.\vec E=\frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon r^2}\hat r.E=4πϵr2q​r^.

For many point charges,

E⃗(r⃗)=∑nqn4πϵRn2R^n.\vec E(\vec r)=\sum_n \frac{q_n}{4\pi\epsilon R_n^2}\hat R_n.E(r)=n∑​4πϵRn2​qn​​R^n​.

For continuous charge,

E⃗(r⃗)=∫ρ(r⃗′)4πϵR2R^ dℓ′, dS′, or dv′,\vec E(\vec r)=\int \frac{\rho(\vec r')}{4\pi\epsilon R^2}\hat R\,d\ell',\ dS',\ \text{or}\ dv',E(r)=∫4πϵR2ρ(r′)​R^dℓ′, dS′, or dv′,

with the proper density and source element.

Electric potential difference is related to the field by

Vab=V(a)−V(b)=−∫baE⃗⋅dl⃗.V_{ab}=V(a)-V(b)=-\int_b^a \vec E\cdot d\vec l.Vab​=V(a)−V(b)=−∫ba​E⋅dl.

If the reference is infinity, the potential of a point charge is

V(r)=q4πϵr.V(r)=\frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon r}.V(r)=4πϵrq​.

The electrostatic field is conservative:

∇×E⃗=0,E⃗=−∇V.\nabla\times\vec E=0,\qquad \vec E=-\nabla V.∇×E=0,E=−∇V.

The zero-curl statement has an important qualification: it holds for electrostatics, not for time-varying magnetic fields. Once ∂B⃗/∂t\partial\vec B/\partial t∂B/∂t is nonzero, Faraday's law gives ∇×E⃗=−∂B⃗/∂t\nabla\times\vec E=-\partial\vec B/\partial t∇×E=−∂B/∂t, and a single-valued scalar potential is not enough to describe the entire electric field. Electrostatic potential methods are therefore powerful but belong to the static limit or to quasistatic cases where induction is negligible.

Key results​

The superposition principle is the workhorse of electrostatics. Because the governing equations are linear in charge and field for ordinary media with constant ϵ\epsilonϵ, fields and potentials from separate sources add. It is often easier to add scalar potentials and then take a gradient than to add vector fields directly:

V(r⃗)=∫ρ(r⃗′)4πϵR dv′,E⃗=−∇V.V(\vec r)=\int \frac{\rho(\vec r')}{4\pi\epsilon R}\,dv', \qquad \vec E=-\nabla V.V(r)=∫4πϵRρ(r′)​dv′,E=−∇V.

For electrostatics in a homogeneous region,

∇⋅D⃗=ρv,D⃗=ϵE⃗.\nabla\cdot\vec D=\rho_v,\qquad \vec D=\epsilon\vec E.∇⋅D=ρv​,D=ϵE.

Combining this with E⃗=−∇V\vec E=-\nabla VE=−∇V gives Poisson's equation:

∇2V=−ρvϵ.\nabla^2V=-\frac{\rho_v}{\epsilon}.∇2V=−ϵρv​​.

In charge-free regions, it reduces to Laplace's equation:

∇2V=0.\nabla^2V=0.∇2V=0.

Potential energy of a point charge in a potential is

W=qV.W=qV.W=qV.

The work done by the field moving charge qqq from aaa to bbb is

Wfield=q[V(a)−V(b)].W_{\text{field}}=q[V(a)-V(b)].Wfield​=q[V(a)−V(b)].

This sign convention is a common source of confusion. A positive charge naturally moves toward lower potential because its potential energy decreases.

The electric dipole is a useful far-field approximation for two equal and opposite charges separated by small distance. Its moment is

p⃗=qd⃗,\vec p=q\vec d,p​=qd,

directed from negative to positive charge. Far from the dipole, the potential falls as 1/r21/r^21/r2 rather than 1/r1/r1/r, because the net charge is zero.

Potential is often the better design variable because conductor surfaces in electrostatic equilibrium are equipotentials. If the conductor voltages are known, a boundary-value problem for VVV can be solved first, then E⃗=−∇V\vec E=-\nabla VE=−∇V gives the field. This sequence is used in capacitance extraction, insulation grading, electrostatic shielding, and semiconductor device approximations. The field is the physically force-producing quantity, but the potential is frequently the simpler mathematical unknown.

Dimensional checking is especially helpful in Coulomb integrals. A line charge contribution ρldl/(4πϵR2)\rho_l dl/(4\pi\epsilon R^2)ρl​dl/(4πϵR2) has units of V/m after multiplying by R^\hat RR^, while a potential contribution ρldl/(4πϵR)\rho_l dl/(4\pi\epsilon R)ρl​dl/(4πϵR) has units of volts. If a result for electric field scales as 1/R1/R1/R for an isolated point charge, or a result for potential scales as 1/R21/R^21/R2, the integration or geometry has likely been mixed up.

The choice between field-first and potential-first methods is usually dictated by symmetry. If the vector directions from all source elements combine simply, a direct E⃗\vec EE integral is efficient. If directions vary but distances are simple, a scalar potential integral may be shorter. If conductor boundaries are specified by voltages, solving for VVV is usually the natural route.

Electrostatic uniqueness is a powerful checking principle. If the potential satisfies the governing equation in the region and satisfies the specified conductor potentials or surface-charge conditions, then no second different electrostatic solution exists for the same problem. This is why boundary-value solutions can be trusted even when the intermediate mathematics looks indirect.

Worked example 1: Field of two point charges on an axis​

Problem: Charges q1=4 nCq_1=4\ \mathrm{nC}q1​=4 nC at x=0x=0x=0 and q2=−1 nCq_2=-1\ \mathrm{nC}q2​=−1 nC at x=3x=3x=3 cm are in free space. Find E⃗\vec EE at x=6x=6x=6 cm.

Step 1: The observation point is to the right of both charges. Distances are

R1=0.06 m,R2=0.03 m.R_1=0.06\ \mathrm{m},\qquad R_2=0.03\ \mathrm{m}.R1​=0.06 m,R2​=0.03 m.

Step 2: Field from q1q_1q1​ points in +x^+\hat x+x^ because q1q_1q1​ is positive and the observation point is to its right:

E⃗1=14πϵ04×10−9(0.06)2x^.\vec E_1=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{4\times10^{-9}}{(0.06)^2}\hat x.E1​=4πϵ0​1​(0.06)24×10−9​x^.

Using 1/(4πϵ0)=8.99×1091/(4\pi\epsilon_0)=8.99\times10^91/(4πϵ0​)=8.99×109,

E⃗1=8.99×1094×10−90.0036x^=9.99×103x^ V/m.\vec E_1=8.99\times10^9\frac{4\times10^{-9}}{0.0036}\hat x =9.99\times10^3\hat x\ \mathrm{V/m}.E1​=8.99×1090.00364×10−9​x^=9.99×103x^ V/m.

Step 3: Field from q2q_2q2​ points toward the negative charge, so at x=6x=6x=6 cm it points in −x^-\hat x−x^:

E⃗2=−8.99×1091×10−9(0.03)2x^=−9.99×103x^ V/m.\vec E_2=-8.99\times10^9\frac{1\times10^{-9}}{(0.03)^2}\hat x =-9.99\times10^3\hat x\ \mathrm{V/m}.E2​=−8.99×109(0.03)21×10−9​x^=−9.99×103x^ V/m.

E⃗=E⃗1+E⃗2=0.\vec E=\vec E_1+\vec E_2=0.E=E1​+E2​=0.

Worked example 2: Potential and field of a uniformly charged ring on its axis​

Problem: A ring of radius aaa carries total charge QQQ uniformly. Find VVV and EzE_zEz​ at a point on the axis a distance zzz from the center, using infinity as reference.

Step 1: Every ring element is the same distance from the observation point:

R=a2+z2.R=\sqrt{a^2+z^2}.R=a2+z2​.

Step 2: The potential contribution is

dV=dq4πϵR.dV=\frac{dq}{4\pi\epsilon R}.dV=4πϵRdq​.

Step 3: Integrate around the ring. Since RRR is constant,

V(z)=14πϵR∫dq=Q4πϵa2+z2.V(z)=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon R}\int dq =\frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon\sqrt{a^2+z^2}}.V(z)=4πϵR1​∫dq=4πϵa2+z2​Q​.

Step 4: Use E⃗=−∇V\vec E=-\nabla VE=−∇V. On the axis, only zzz variation remains:

Ez=−dVdz.E_z=-\frac{dV}{dz}.Ez​=−dzdV​.

Step 5: Differentiate:

ddz(a2+z2)−1/2=−12(a2+z2)−3/2(2z)=−z(a2+z2)−3/2.\frac{d}{dz}(a^2+z^2)^{-1/2} =-\frac{1}{2}(a^2+z^2)^{-3/2}(2z) =-z(a^2+z^2)^{-3/2}.dzd​(a2+z2)−1/2=−21​(a2+z2)−3/2(2z)=−z(a2+z2)−3/2.

Thus

Ez=Qz4πϵ(a2+z2)3/2.E_z=\frac{Qz}{4\pi\epsilon(a^2+z^2)^{3/2}}.Ez​=4πϵ(a2+z2)3/2Qz​.

Check: At z=0z=0z=0, Ez=0E_z=0Ez​=0 by symmetry. Far away, Ez≈Q/(4πϵz2)E_z\approx Q/(4\pi\epsilon z^2)Ez​≈Q/(4πϵz2), like a point charge.

Code​

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

eps0 = 8.8541878128e-12
Q = 1e-9
a = 0.05
z = np.linspace(-0.2, 0.2, 500)

V = Q / (4 * np.pi * eps0 * np.sqrt(a**2 + z**2))
Ez = Q * z / (4 * np.pi * eps0 * (a**2 + z**2)**1.5)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(2, 1, sharex=True)
ax[0].plot(z, V)
ax[0].set_ylabel("V (volts)")
ax[0].grid(True)
ax[1].plot(z, Ez)
ax[1].set_xlabel("z (m)")
ax[1].set_ylabel("Ez (V/m)")
ax[1].grid(True)
plt.show()

Common pitfalls​

  • Using R⃗\vec RR in the wrong direction. In field integrals, R⃗\vec RR points from source to observation point.
  • Forgetting that potential is scalar. Add potentials algebraically, then compute E⃗=−∇V\vec E=-\nabla VE=−∇V if needed.
  • Dropping the sign in E⃗=−∇V\vec E=-\nabla VE=−∇V. The electric field points toward decreasing potential for positive charge.
  • Applying point-charge formulas inside a continuous charge distribution without integrating.
  • Using ϵ0\epsilon_0ϵ0​ when the charge is embedded in another homogeneous dielectric with ϵ=ϵrϵ0\epsilon=\epsilon_r\epsilon_0ϵ=ϵr​ϵ0​.
  • Ignoring units for charge density. Line, surface, and volume densities use different dimensions.
  • Treating the potential reference as physically measurable. Only potential differences affect work and circuit voltage.
