Vector Algebra and Coordinate Systems

Electromagnetics is a field theory, so most quantities have direction as well as magnitude. The electric field E⃗\vec EE, magnetic field intensity H⃗\vec HH, flux densities D⃗\vec DD and B⃗\vec BB, current density J⃗\vec JJ, force F⃗\vec FF, and area vector dS⃗d\vec SdS are all vectors. Before differentiating or integrating fields, we need reliable notation for positions, distances, unit vectors, dot products, cross products, and coordinate transformations.

The practical reason to learn multiple coordinate systems is symmetry. A line charge is natural in cylindrical coordinates, a point charge is natural in spherical coordinates, and a rectangular waveguide is natural in Cartesian coordinates. Choosing coordinates that match the geometry usually turns a hard integral into a short calculation. This page supports the vector calculus page and connects directly to vector differential calculus.

Definitions​

A vector A⃗\vec AA has magnitude ∣A⃗∣\vert \vec A\vert ∣A∣ and direction. In Cartesian coordinates,

A⃗=Axx^+Ayy^+Azz^.\vec A=A_x\hat x+A_y\hat y+A_z\hat z.A=Ax​x^+Ay​y^​+Az​z^.

The dot product measures projection:

A⃗⋅B⃗=∣A⃗∣∣B⃗∣cos⁡θ=AxBx+AyBy+AzBz.\vec A\cdot \vec B=|\vec A||\vec B|\cos\theta=A_xB_x+A_yB_y+A_zB_z.A⋅B=∣A∣∣B∣cosθ=Ax​Bx​+Ay​By​+Az​Bz​.

The cross product creates a vector normal to the plane of A⃗\vec AA and B⃗\vec BB:

A⃗×B⃗=∣x^y^z^AxAyAzBxByBz∣.\vec A\times \vec B= \begin{vmatrix} \hat x & \hat y & \hat z\\ A_x & A_y & A_z\\ B_x & B_y & B_z \end{vmatrix}.A×B=​x^Ax​Bx​​y^​Ay​By​​z^Az​Bz​​​.

Its magnitude is ∣A⃗∣∣B⃗∣sin⁡θ\vert \vec A\vert \vert \vec B\vert \sin\theta∣A∣∣B∣sinθ, and its direction follows the right-hand rule.

The position vector in Cartesian coordinates is

r⃗=xx^+yy^+zz^.\vec r=x\hat x+y\hat y+z\hat z.r=xx^+yy^​+zz^.

In cylindrical coordinates (ρ,ϕ,z)(\rho,\phi,z)(ρ,ϕ,z),

r⃗=ρρ^+zz^,x=ρcos⁡ϕ,y=ρsin⁡ϕ.\vec r=\rho\hat \rho+z\hat z,\qquad x=\rho\cos\phi,\qquad y=\rho\sin\phi.r=ρρ^​+zz^,x=ρcosϕ,y=ρsinϕ.

In spherical coordinates (r,θ,ϕ)(r,\theta,\phi)(r,θ,ϕ), where θ\thetaθ is measured from +z+z+z and ϕ\phiϕ in the xxx-yyy plane,

x=rsin⁡θcos⁡ϕ,y=rsin⁡θsin⁡ϕ,z=rcos⁡θ.x=r\sin\theta\cos\phi,\qquad y=r\sin\theta\sin\phi,\qquad z=r\cos\theta.x=rsinθcosϕ,y=rsinθsinϕ,z=rcosθ.

The differential lengths are

dl⃗cart=dxx^+dyy^+dzz^,dl⃗cyl=dρρ^+ρdϕϕ^+dzz^,dl⃗sph=drr^+rdθθ^+rsin⁡θdϕϕ^.\begin{aligned} d\vec l_{\text{cart}} &= dx\hat x+dy\hat y+dz\hat z,\\ d\vec l_{\text{cyl}} &= d\rho\hat\rho+\rho d\phi\hat\phi+dz\hat z,\\ d\vec l_{\text{sph}} &= dr\hat r+r d\theta\hat\theta+r\sin\theta d\phi\hat\phi. \end{aligned}dlcart​dlcyl​dlsph​​=dxx^+dyy^​+dzz^,=dρρ^​+ρdϕϕ^​+dzz^,=drr^+rdθθ^+rsinθdϕϕ^​.​

The corresponding volume elements are

dvcart=dx dy dz,dvcyl=ρ dρ dϕ dz,dvsph=r2sin⁡θ dr dθ dϕ.dv_{\text{cart}}=dx\,dy\,dz,\qquad dv_{\text{cyl}}=\rho\,d\rho\,d\phi\,dz,\qquad dv_{\text{sph}}=r^2\sin\theta\,dr\,d\theta\,d\phi.dvcart​=dxdydz,dvcyl​=ρdρdϕdz,dvsph​=r2sinθdrdθdϕ.

These extra factors are scale factors. Cartesian coordinates have scale factors 1,1,11,1,11,1,1. Cylindrical coordinates have 1,ρ,11,\rho,11,ρ,1, because moving through a small azimuthal angle dϕd\phidϕ traces an arc length ρdϕ\rho d\phiρdϕ. Spherical coordinates have 1,r,rsin⁡θ1,r,r\sin\theta1,r,rsinθ, because angular motion traces arcs whose radii depend on position. Whenever an integral looks suspiciously too simple in cylindrical or spherical coordinates, the missing scale factor is the first thing to check.

Coordinate singularities are not physical singularities by themselves. At ρ=0\rho=0ρ=0, the unit vectors ρ^\hat\rhoρ^​ and ϕ^\hat\phiϕ^​ are not uniquely defined because every azimuth points to the same axis. At r=0r=0r=0, spherical angular directions are undefined. A field may be perfectly finite at these locations even if the coordinate description is awkward, so distinguish coordinate artifacts from actual point sources or infinite field values.

Key results​

Coordinate unit vectors in curvilinear systems depend on position. In cylindrical coordinates,

ρ^=cos⁡ϕx^+sin⁡ϕy^,ϕ^=−sin⁡ϕx^+cos⁡ϕy^.\hat \rho=\cos\phi\hat x+\sin\phi\hat y,\qquad \hat \phi=-\sin\phi\hat x+\cos\phi\hat y.ρ^​=cosϕx^+sinϕy^​,ϕ^​=−sinϕx^+cosϕy^​.

In spherical coordinates,

r^=sin⁡θcos⁡ϕx^+sin⁡θsin⁡ϕy^+cos⁡θz^,θ^=cos⁡θcos⁡ϕx^+cos⁡θsin⁡ϕy^−sin⁡θz^,ϕ^=−sin⁡ϕx^+cos⁡ϕy^.\begin{aligned} \hat r &= \sin\theta\cos\phi\hat x+\sin\theta\sin\phi\hat y+\cos\theta\hat z,\\ \hat \theta &= \cos\theta\cos\phi\hat x+\cos\theta\sin\phi\hat y-\sin\theta\hat z,\\ \hat \phi &= -\sin\phi\hat x+\cos\phi\hat y. \end{aligned}r^θ^ϕ^​​=sinθcosϕx^+sinθsinϕy^​+cosθz^,=cosθcosϕx^+cosθsinϕy^​−sinθz^,=−sinϕx^+cosϕy^​.​

Distance vectors should be built carefully. If a source point is r⃗′\vec r'r′ and an observation point is r⃗\vec rr, the separation vector is

R⃗=r⃗−r⃗′,\vec R=\vec r-\vec r',R=r−r′,

with magnitude R=∣R⃗∣R=\vert \vec R\vert R=∣R∣ and direction R^=R⃗/R\hat R=\vec R/RR^=R/R. Coulomb and Biot-Savart integrals repeatedly use this construction. A common error is to subtract coordinates in cylindrical or spherical form without accounting for changing unit vectors; when in doubt, convert to Cartesian components, subtract, then convert back if useful.

For areas, the vector direction is essential. Examples include

dS⃗ρ=ρdϕdz ρ^,dS⃗r=r2sin⁡θdθdϕ r^.d\vec S_{\rho}=\rho d\phi dz\,\hat\rho,\qquad d\vec S_{r}=r^2\sin\theta d\theta d\phi\,\hat r.dSρ​=ρdϕdzρ^​,dSr​=r2sinθdθdϕr^.

The sign of flux integrals depends on choosing the outward normal for closed surfaces or the normal specified by the right-hand rule for open surfaces bounded by a contour.

The unit-vector transformations also provide a reliable way to transform vector components. A physical vector does not change when coordinates change, but its component list does. For example, a vector in the xxx direction has cylindrical components that vary with ϕ\phiϕ because

x^=cos⁡ϕρ^−sin⁡ϕϕ^.\hat x=\cos\phi\hat\rho-\sin\phi\hat\phi.x^=cosϕρ^​−sinϕϕ^​.

This position dependence explains why differentiating vector fields in cylindrical and spherical coordinates is more complicated than differentiating their scalar component functions.

Triple products appear often in force and power manipulations:

A⃗⋅(B⃗×C⃗)=B⃗⋅(C⃗×A⃗),\vec A\cdot(\vec B\times\vec C)=\vec B\cdot(\vec C\times\vec A),A⋅(B×C)=B⋅(C×A),

and

A⃗×(B⃗×C⃗)=B⃗(A⃗⋅C⃗)−C⃗(A⃗⋅B⃗).\vec A\times(\vec B\times\vec C)=\vec B(\vec A\cdot\vec C)-\vec C(\vec A\cdot\vec B).A×(B×C)=B(A⋅C)−C(A⋅B).

These identities are not decorative; they simplify torque, magnetic force, and Poynting vector expressions.

As a final check, every coordinate choice should preserve physical length, area, volume, and direction. If two coordinate descriptions give different distances or fluxes for the same geometry, the error is in the representation, not in the physics.

Worked example 1: Distance vector in cylindrical coordinates​

Problem: A source point is at (ρ′,ϕ′,z′)=(2,30∘,1)(\rho',\phi',z')=(2,30^\circ,1)(ρ′,ϕ′,z′)=(2,30∘,1) m and an observation point is at (ρ,ϕ,z)=(5,120∘,4)(\rho,\phi,z)=(5,120^\circ,4)(ρ,ϕ,z)=(5,120∘,4) m. Find R⃗=r⃗−r⃗′\vec R=\vec r-\vec r'R=r−r′ and RRR.

Step 1: Convert each point to Cartesian coordinates. For the observation point,

x=5cos⁡120∘=−2.5,y=5sin⁡120∘=4.330,z=4.\begin{aligned} x&=5\cos120^\circ=-2.5,\\ y&=5\sin120^\circ=4.330,\\ z&=4. \end{aligned}xyz​=5cos120∘=−2.5,=5sin120∘=4.330,=4.​

For the source point,

x′=2cos⁡30∘=1.732,y′=2sin⁡30∘=1.000,z′=1.\begin{aligned} x'&=2\cos30^\circ=1.732,\\ y'&=2\sin30^\circ=1.000,\\ z'&=1. \end{aligned}x′y′z′​=2cos30∘=1.732,=2sin30∘=1.000,=1.​

Step 2: Subtract coordinates:

R⃗=(−2.5−1.732)x^+(4.330−1.000)y^+(4−1)z^.\vec R=(-2.5-1.732)\hat x+(4.330-1.000)\hat y+(4-1)\hat z.R=(−2.5−1.732)x^+(4.330−1.000)y^​+(4−1)z^.

Thus

R⃗=−4.232x^+3.330y^+3z^ m.\vec R=-4.232\hat x+3.330\hat y+3\hat z\ \mathrm{m}.R=−4.232x^+3.330y^​+3z^ m.

Step 3: Compute magnitude:

R=(−4.232)2+(3.330)2+32=6.16 m.R=\sqrt{(-4.232)^2+(3.330)^2+3^2}=6.16\ \mathrm{m}.R=(−4.232)2+(3.330)2+32​=6.16 m.

Check: The vertical difference alone is 333 m, so a total distance of 6.166.166.16 m is plausible.

Worked example 2: Surface area of a spherical cap​

Problem: Find the area of the spherical surface r=ar=ar=a between 0≤θ≤θ00\le\theta\le\theta_00≤θ≤θ0​ and 0≤ϕ≤2π0\le\phi\le2\pi0≤ϕ≤2π.

Step 1: Use the spherical area element normal to r^\hat rr^:

dS=a2sin⁡θ dθ dϕ.dS=a^2\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi.dS=a2sinθdθdϕ.

Step 2: Integrate over the cap:

S=∫02π∫0θ0a2sin⁡θ dθ dϕ.S=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\theta_0}a^2\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi.S=∫02π​∫0θ0​​a2sinθdθdϕ.

Step 3: Evaluate the inner integral:

∫0θ0sin⁡θ dθ=[−cos⁡θ]0θ0=1−cos⁡θ0.\int_0^{\theta_0}\sin\theta\,d\theta =\left[-\cos\theta\right]_0^{\theta_0} =1-\cos\theta_0.∫0θ0​​sinθdθ=[−cosθ]0θ0​​=1−cosθ0​.

Step 4: Evaluate the outer integral:

S=2πa2(1−cos⁡θ0).S=2\pi a^2(1-\cos\theta_0).S=2πa2(1−cosθ0​).

Check: If θ0=π\theta_0=\piθ0​=π, the cap is the full sphere and S=2πa2(1−cos⁡π)=4πa2S=2\pi a^2(1-\cos\pi)=4\pi a^2S=2πa2(1−cosπ)=4πa2.

Code​

import numpy as np

def cyl_to_cart(rho, phi_deg, z):
    phi = np.deg2rad(phi_deg)
    return np.array([rho * np.cos(phi), rho * np.sin(phi), z], dtype=float)

source = cyl_to_cart(2, 30, 1)
obs = cyl_to_cart(5, 120, 4)
R_vec = obs - source
R_mag = np.linalg.norm(R_vec)

print("source =", source)
print("observer =", obs)
print("R vector =", R_vec)
print("R magnitude =", R_mag)

Common pitfalls​

  • Treating ρ^\hat\rhoρ^​, ϕ^\hat\phiϕ^​, r^\hat rr^, and θ^\hat\thetaθ^ as constant everywhere. They rotate with position.
  • Omitting metric factors such as ρ\rhoρ, rrr, and rsin⁡θr\sin\thetarsinθ from differential lengths, areas, or volumes.
  • Using θ\thetaθ as the azimuth angle in spherical coordinates. In the convention used here, θ\thetaθ is measured down from +z+z+z and ϕ\phiϕ is azimuth.
  • Forgetting that dS⃗d\vec SdS has direction. Flux signs depend on the chosen normal.
  • Subtracting curvilinear coordinate triples directly to form distance vectors.
  • Reversing cross-product order. A⃗×B⃗=−(B⃗×A⃗)\vec A\times\vec B=-(\vec B\times\vec A)A×B=−(B×A).
  • Assuming a coordinate transformation changes the physical vector. Only the basis and component representation change.
