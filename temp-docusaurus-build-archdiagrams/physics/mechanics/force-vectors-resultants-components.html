Force Vectors, Resultants, and Components

Engineering mechanics starts by treating force as a vector: it has magnitude, direction, and a line of action. That one decision lets a single notation describe cable tensions, weights, contact reactions, distributed loads after reduction, and the resultant force from many applied effects. The same vector language also describes relative position, so forces and geometry can be combined later into moments.

This page builds the vector habits used everywhere else in statics and dynamics. Before drawing an equilibrium equation or an equation of motion, you usually resolve forces into components, add them into a resultant, and check whether the answer makes physical sense. A clean vector calculation is not cosmetic; it is what prevents sign errors, wrong angles, and accidental mixing of scalars with vectors.

Definitions​

A scalar has magnitude only. Mass, distance, time, speed, work, and energy are scalars. A vector has magnitude and direction. Force F\mathbf{F}F, position r\mathbf{r}r, velocity v\mathbf{v}v, acceleration a\mathbf{a}a, and moment M\mathbf{M}M are vectors. In Cartesian components,

F=Fxi+Fyj+Fzk\mathbf{F}=F_x\mathbf{i}+F_y\mathbf{j}+F_z\mathbf{k}F=Fx​i+Fy​j+Fz​k

where i\mathbf{i}i, j\mathbf{j}j, and k\mathbf{k}k are unit basis vectors along the xxx, yyy, and zzz axes. The magnitude is

∣F∣=Fx2+Fy2+Fz2.|\mathbf{F}|=\sqrt{F_x^2+F_y^2+F_z^2}.∣F∣=Fx2​+Fy2​+Fz2​​.

A unit vector has magnitude one. If a force of magnitude FFF acts along a line from point AAA to point BBB, first form the direction vector

rAB=(xB−xA)i+(yB−yA)j+(zB−zA)k,\mathbf{r}_{AB}=(x_B-x_A)\mathbf{i}+(y_B-y_A)\mathbf{j}+(z_B-z_A)\mathbf{k},rAB​=(xB​−xA​)i+(yB​−yA​)j+(zB​−zA​)k,

then normalize it:

uAB=rAB∣rAB∣.\mathbf{u}_{AB}=\frac{\mathbf{r}_{AB}}{|\mathbf{r}_{AB}|}.uAB​=∣rAB​∣rAB​​.

The force vector is then

F=FuAB.\mathbf{F}=F\mathbf{u}_{AB}.F=FuAB​.

A resultant force is a single force equal to the vector sum of a system of forces:

R=∑iFi.\mathbf{R}=\sum_i\mathbf{F}_i.R=i∑​Fi​.

If all forces act at the same point, the resultant can replace them for force balance. If forces act at different points on a rigid body, the same resultant force alone may not preserve the moment effect; later pages add the associated moment.

The components of a force are the signed scalar multipliers of the basis vectors. In 2D, if a force FFF makes an angle θ\thetaθ counterclockwise from the positive xxx axis, then

Fx=Fcos⁡θ,Fy=Fsin⁡θ.F_x=F\cos\theta,\qquad F_y=F\sin\theta.Fx​=Fcosθ,Fy​=Fsinθ.

If the angle is measured from another axis, do not blindly use cosine for xxx and sine for yyy. Draw the right triangle, attach signs from the chosen axes, and only then write the components.

The dot product measures projection:

A⋅B=∣A∣∣B∣cos⁡θ=AxBx+AyBy+AzBz.\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B}=|\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|\cos\theta=A_xB_x+A_yB_y+A_zB_z.A⋅B=∣A∣∣B∣cosθ=Ax​Bx​+Ay​By​+Az​Bz​.

The scalar component of F\mathbf{F}F along a unit direction u\mathbf{u}u is F⋅u\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{u}F⋅u. The vector projection is (F⋅u)u(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{u})\mathbf{u}(F⋅u)u.

The cross product produces a vector perpendicular to two vectors:

A×B=∣ijkAxAyAzBxByBz∣.\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}= \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k}\\ A_x & A_y & A_z\\ B_x & B_y & B_z \end{vmatrix}.A×B=​iAx​Bx​​jAy​By​​kAz​Bz​​​.

For mechanics, the cross product matters because the moment of a force about a point is M=r×F\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F}M=r×F.

Key results​

Vector addition is component-wise:

∑iFi=(∑iFix)i+(∑iFiy)j+(∑iFiz)k.\sum_i\mathbf{F}_i= \left(\sum_i F_{ix}\right)\mathbf{i} +\left(\sum_i F_{iy}\right)\mathbf{j} +\left(\sum_i F_{iz}\right)\mathbf{k}.i∑​Fi​=(i∑​Fix​)i+(i∑​Fiy​)j+(i∑​Fiz​)k.

This is why most force problems become arithmetic after a good diagram. A force polygon may show the geometry, but component equations compute the answer reliably. In 2D,

Rx=∑iFix,Ry=∑iFiy,R=Rx2+Ry2,θ=tan⁡−1(RyRx)R_x=\sum_iF_{ix},\qquad R_y=\sum_iF_{iy},\qquad R=\sqrt{R_x^2+R_y^2},\qquad \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{R_y}{R_x}\right)Rx​=i∑​Fix​,Ry​=i∑​Fiy​,R=Rx2​+Ry2​​,θ=tan−1(Rx​Ry​​)

with the angle placed in the correct quadrant. In code, use atan2(R_y, R_x) rather than a plain arctangent.

For 3D cable or link problems, unit-vector construction is usually safer than angle memorization. If a cable force T\mathbf{T}T pulls from AAA toward BBB, use

T=TrAB∣rAB∣.\mathbf{T}=T\frac{\mathbf{r}_{AB}}{|\mathbf{r}_{AB}|}.T=T∣rAB​∣rAB​​.

This automatically gives all three component signs. The point order matters: rAB\mathbf{r}_{AB}rAB​ points from AAA to BBB, while rBA\mathbf{r}_{BA}rBA​ points from BBB to AAA.

Two common checks catch many errors. First, each component must be no larger in magnitude than the force magnitude:

∣Fx∣≤F,∣Fy∣≤F,∣Fz∣≤F.|F_x|\le F,\qquad |F_y|\le F,\qquad |F_z|\le F.∣Fx​∣≤F,∣Fy​∣≤F,∣Fz​∣≤F.

Second, the components must reconstruct the magnitude:

Fx2+Fy2+Fz2=F2.F_x^2+F_y^2+F_z^2=F^2.Fx2​+Fy2​+Fz2​=F2.

For a set of concurrent forces, equilibrium means R=0\mathbf{R}=\mathbf{0}R=0. For a set of nonconcurrent forces on a rigid body, R\mathbf{R}R is still important, but not complete. The force system also has a moment about any chosen point:

MO=∑irOi×Fi.\mathbf{M}_O=\sum_i\mathbf{r}_{Oi}\times\mathbf{F}_i.MO​=i∑​rOi​×Fi​.

Later pages use the pair (R,MO)(\mathbf{R},\mathbf{M}_O)(R,MO​) to represent equivalent force systems. The key point here is that a vector calculation has a location part and a direction part. Treating a force as only a magnitude and an angle loses information needed for moments.

Worked example 1: Resultant of three planar forces​

Problem. Three forces act at a pin: 808080 N at 30∘30^\circ30∘ above the positive xxx axis, 555555 N at 120∘120^\circ120∘ from the positive xxx axis, and 404040 N downward. Find the resultant magnitude and direction.

Method. Resolve each force into xxx and yyy components, add components, then convert the resultant back to magnitude and angle.

  1. Write the first force:
F1=80cos⁡30∘ i+80sin⁡30∘ j.\mathbf{F}_1=80\cos30^\circ\,\mathbf{i}+80\sin30^\circ\,\mathbf{j}.F1​=80cos30∘i+80sin30∘j.

Using cos⁡30∘=0.8660\cos30^\circ=0.8660cos30∘=0.8660 and sin⁡30∘=0.5\sin30^\circ=0.5sin30∘=0.5,

F1=69.28i+40.00j N.\mathbf{F}_1=69.28\mathbf{i}+40.00\mathbf{j}\ \text{N}.F1​=69.28i+40.00j N.
  1. Write the second force:
F2=55cos⁡120∘ i+55sin⁡120∘ j.\mathbf{F}_2=55\cos120^\circ\,\mathbf{i}+55\sin120^\circ\,\mathbf{j}.F2​=55cos120∘i+55sin120∘j.

Since cos⁡120∘=−0.5\cos120^\circ=-0.5cos120∘=−0.5 and sin⁡120∘=0.8660\sin120^\circ=0.8660sin120∘=0.8660,

F2=−27.50i+47.63j N.\mathbf{F}_2=-27.50\mathbf{i}+47.63\mathbf{j}\ \text{N}.F2​=−27.50i+47.63j N.
  1. Write the vertical force:
F3=0i−40.00j N.\mathbf{F}_3=0\mathbf{i}-40.00\mathbf{j}\ \text{N}.F3​=0i−40.00j N.
  1. Add components:
Rx=69.28−27.50+0=41.78 N,Ry=40.00+47.63−40.00=47.63 N.\begin{aligned} R_x&=69.28-27.50+0=41.78\ \text{N},\\ R_y&=40.00+47.63-40.00=47.63\ \text{N}. \end{aligned}Rx​Ry​​=69.28−27.50+0=41.78 N,=40.00+47.63−40.00=47.63 N.​

Thus

R=41.78i+47.63j N.\mathbf{R}=41.78\mathbf{i}+47.63\mathbf{j}\ \text{N}.R=41.78i+47.63j N.
  1. Compute magnitude and direction:
R=41.782+47.632=63.36 N.R=\sqrt{41.78^2+47.63^2}=63.36\ \text{N}.R=41.782+47.632​=63.36 N. θ=tan⁡−1(47.6341.78)=48.7∘.\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{47.63}{41.78}\right)=48.7^\circ.θ=tan−1(41.7847.63​)=48.7∘.

Both components are positive, so the direction is in quadrant I. The checked answer is

R=41.78i+47.63j N,R=63.36 N at 48.7∘.\boxed{\mathbf{R}=41.78\mathbf{i}+47.63\mathbf{j}\ \text{N},\quad R=63.36\ \text{N at }48.7^\circ.}R=41.78i+47.63j N,R=63.36 N at 48.7∘.​

The magnitude is less than 80+55+40=17580+55+40=17580+55+40=175 N, which is plausible because the forces partly oppose each other.

Worked example 2: Cable force in 3D​

Problem. A cable runs from point A=(1,2,0)A=(1,2,0)A=(1,2,0) m to point B=(4,−1,6)B=(4,-1,6)B=(4,−1,6) m. The cable tension is 240240240 N and pulls on the attachment at AAA toward BBB. Find the Cartesian force vector at AAA.

Method. Build the direction vector from AAA to BBB, normalize it, then multiply by the tension magnitude.

  1. Compute the relative position vector:
rAB=(4−1)i+(−1−2)j+(6−0)k=3i−3j+6k m.\mathbf{r}_{AB}=(4-1)\mathbf{i}+(-1-2)\mathbf{j}+(6-0)\mathbf{k} =3\mathbf{i}-3\mathbf{j}+6\mathbf{k}\ \text{m}.rAB​=(4−1)i+(−1−2)j+(6−0)k=3i−3j+6k m.
  1. Compute its length:
∣rAB∣=32+(−3)2+62=54=7.348 m.|\mathbf{r}_{AB}|=\sqrt{3^2+(-3)^2+6^2} =\sqrt{54}=7.348\ \text{m}.∣rAB​∣=32+(−3)2+62​=54​=7.348 m.
  1. Form the unit vector:
uAB=37.348i−37.348j+67.348k=0.4082i−0.4082j+0.8165k.\mathbf{u}_{AB}=\frac{3}{7.348}\mathbf{i}-\frac{3}{7.348}\mathbf{j}+\frac{6}{7.348}\mathbf{k} =0.4082\mathbf{i}-0.4082\mathbf{j}+0.8165\mathbf{k}.uAB​=7.3483​i−7.3483​j+7.3486​k=0.4082i−0.4082j+0.8165k.
  1. Multiply by the tension:
T=240uAB=97.98i−97.98j+195.96k N.\mathbf{T}=240\mathbf{u}_{AB} =97.98\mathbf{i}-97.98\mathbf{j}+195.96\mathbf{k}\ \text{N}.T=240uAB​=97.98i−97.98j+195.96k N.
  1. Check the magnitude:
97.982+(−97.98)2+195.962=240.0 N.\sqrt{97.98^2+(-97.98)^2+195.96^2}=240.0\ \text{N}.97.982+(−97.98)2+195.962​=240.0 N.

The checked answer is

T=98.0i−98.0j+196.0k N.\boxed{\mathbf{T}=98.0\mathbf{i}-98.0\mathbf{j}+196.0\mathbf{k}\ \text{N}.}T=98.0i−98.0j+196.0k N.​

The negative yyy component is not an algebra mistake; the cable goes from y=2y=2y=2 to y=−1y=-1y=−1, so it pulls toward decreasing yyy.

Code​

import math

def components_from_angle(magnitude, degrees):
    theta = math.radians(degrees)
    return magnitude * math.cos(theta), magnitude * math.sin(theta)

forces = [
    components_from_angle(80.0, 30.0),
    components_from_angle(55.0, 120.0),
    (0.0, -40.0),
]

rx = sum(fx for fx, fy in forces)
ry = sum(fy for fx, fy in forces)
resultant = math.hypot(rx, ry)
angle = math.degrees(math.atan2(ry, rx))

print(f"R = ({rx:.2f}, {ry:.2f}) N")
print(f"|R| = {resultant:.2f} N")
print(f"direction = {angle:.2f} degrees")

Common pitfalls​

  • Adding force magnitudes instead of signed components.
  • Using sin⁡\sinsin and cos⁡\coscos from memory without checking which axis the angle is measured from.
  • Forgetting that a cable pulls away from the point being isolated, along the cable.
  • Reversing rAB\mathbf{r}_{AB}rAB​ and rBA\mathbf{r}_{BA}rBA​ in 3D force construction.
  • Reporting tan⁡−1(Ry/Rx)\tan^{-1}(R_y/R_x)tan−1(Ry​/Rx​) without checking the quadrant.
  • Dropping units during component work, especially when geometry is in meters and force is in newtons.
  • Treating a force resultant as a complete rigid-body replacement without also preserving moment.
