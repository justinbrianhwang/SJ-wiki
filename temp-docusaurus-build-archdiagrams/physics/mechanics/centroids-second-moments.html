Centroids and Second Moments

Centroids and second moments connect geometry to mechanics. The centroid locates the balance point of an area, line, volume, or mass distribution. The second moment of area describes how area is spread relative to an axis, which later controls bending stress and beam deflection. The mass moment of inertia describes how mass is spread relative to an axis, which later controls rotational dynamics.

The computations look similar, but the physical meanings differ. A centroid is a first moment divided by a total measure. A second moment is an integral of squared distance. Both reward careful coordinate choice, symmetry recognition, and composite-body bookkeeping.

Definitions​

For an area AAA in the xyxyxy plane, the centroid (xˉ,yˉ)(\bar{x},\bar{y})(xˉ,yˉ​) is

xˉ=1A∫Ax dA,yˉ=1A∫Ay dA.\bar{x}=\frac{1}{A}\int_A x\,dA,\qquad \bar{y}=\frac{1}{A}\int_A y\,dA.xˉ=A1​∫A​xdA,yˉ​=A1​∫A​ydA.

For a composite area made from simple pieces,

xˉ=∑iAixi∑iAi,yˉ=∑iAiyi∑iAi.\bar{x}=\frac{\sum_i A_i x_i}{\sum_i A_i},\qquad \bar{y}=\frac{\sum_i A_i y_i}{\sum_i A_i}.xˉ=∑i​Ai​∑i​Ai​xi​​,yˉ​=∑i​Ai​∑i​Ai​yi​​.

Voids or holes are included as negative areas. The same idea works for mass centers:

xˉ=∑imixi∑imi.\bar{x}=\frac{\sum_i m_i x_i}{\sum_i m_i}.xˉ=∑i​mi​∑i​mi​xi​​.

The first moment of area about the yyy axis is

Qy=∫Ax dA=Axˉ.Q_y=\int_A x\,dA=A\bar{x}.Qy​=∫A​xdA=Axˉ.

The first moment about the xxx axis is

Qx=∫Ay dA=Ayˉ.Q_x=\int_A y\,dA=A\bar{y}.Qx​=∫A​ydA=Ayˉ​.

The second moment of area about the xxx and yyy axes is

Ix=∫Ay2 dA,Iy=∫Ax2 dA.I_x=\int_A y^2\,dA,\qquad I_y=\int_A x^2\,dA.Ix​=∫A​y2dA,Iy​=∫A​x2dA.

The polar second moment of area about point OOO is

JO=∫Ar2 dA=Ix+IyJ_O=\int_A r^2\,dA=I_x+I_yJO​=∫A​r2dA=Ix​+Iy​

when xxx and yyy are perpendicular axes through OOO.

The product of inertia is

Ixy=∫Axy dA.I_{xy}=\int_A xy\,dA.Ixy​=∫A​xydA.

It is zero if either the xxx or yyy axis is an axis of symmetry for the area.

The mass moment of inertia about an axis is

I=∫r2 dm.I=\int r^2\,dm.I=∫r2dm.

Its units are mass times length squared, such as kg m2^22. Do not confuse this with second moment of area, whose units are length to the fourth power.

Key results​

Symmetry is the first result to use. If an area has a line of symmetry, its centroid lies on that line. If it has two perpendicular symmetry axes, their intersection is the centroid. If an area is symmetric about the yyy axis, then Ixy=0I_{xy}=0Ixy​=0 for centroidal axes aligned with that symmetry.

For composite centroid calculations, use signed pieces:

xˉ=A1x1+A2x2−AhxhA1+A2−Ah\bar{x}=\frac{A_1x_1+A_2x_2-A_hx_h}{A_1+A_2-A_h}xˉ=A1​+A2​−Ah​A1​x1​+A2​x2​−Ah​xh​​

when a hole of area AhA_hAh​ is removed. The centroid of a removed region still has coordinates; the negative sign belongs to the area, not to the coordinate unless the coordinate itself is negative.

The parallel-axis theorem for second moments of area states

Ix=Ixc+Ad2,I_x=I_{x_c}+Ad^2,Ix​=Ixc​​+Ad2,

where IxcI_{x_c}Ixc​​ is about a centroidal axis parallel to xxx, and ddd is the perpendicular distance between the axes. This theorem moves from a centroidal axis to a parallel noncentroidal axis. It does not move between rotated axes.

For mass moments,

IO=IG+md2,I_O=I_G+md^2,IO​=IG​+md2,

where GGG is the center of mass and ddd is the perpendicular distance between parallel axes.

Common centroidal second moments of area are:

Ix=bh312for a rectangle about its centroidal horizontal axis,I_x=\frac{bh^3}{12}\quad\text{for a rectangle about its centroidal horizontal axis},Ix​=12bh3​for a rectangle about its centroidal horizontal axis, Iy=hb312for a rectangle about its centroidal vertical axis,I_y=\frac{hb^3}{12}\quad\text{for a rectangle about its centroidal vertical axis},Iy​=12hb3​for a rectangle about its centroidal vertical axis, I=πr44for a circle about a centroidal diameter.I=\frac{\pi r^4}{4}\quad\text{for a circle about a centroidal diameter}.I=4πr4​for a circle about a centroidal diameter.

These formulas should be paired with a sketch of the axis. The same rectangle has different IxI_xIx​ and IyI_yIy​ when b≠hb\ne hb=h, because the squared distance is measured perpendicular to the chosen axis.

For distributed loads and hydrostatic pressure, the resultant location is also a centroid idea. The equivalent force of a uniform distributed load acts at the centroid of the loaded length. A linearly varying triangular load acts one third of the base length from the larger-intensity end, or two thirds from the zero-intensity end.

Worked example 1: Centroid of an L-shaped plate​

Problem. An L-shaped plate is made from a 120120120 mm by 808080 mm rectangle with a 707070 mm by 404040 mm rectangular cutout removed from the upper right corner. The origin is at the lower-left corner of the original rectangle. The remaining shape consists of the full rectangle minus the cutout occupying 50≤x≤12050\le x\le12050≤x≤120 mm and 40≤y≤8040\le y\le8040≤y≤80 mm. Find the centroid.

Method. Treat the plate as a positive large rectangle plus a negative cutout.

  1. Large rectangle:
A1=120(80)=9600 mm2.A_1=120(80)=9600\ \text{mm}^2.A1​=120(80)=9600 mm2.

Its centroid is

(x1,y1)=(60,40) mm.(x_1,y_1)=(60,40)\ \text{mm}.(x1​,y1​)=(60,40) mm.

A2=70(40)=2800 mm2.A_2=70(40)=2800\ \text{mm}^2.A2​=70(40)=2800 mm2.

Its centroid is halfway through the removed region:

x2=50+1202=85 mm,x_2=\frac{50+120}{2}=85\ \text{mm},x2​=250+120​=85 mm, y2=40+802=60 mm.y_2=\frac{40+80}{2}=60\ \text{mm}.y2​=240+80​=60 mm.

Use A2A_2A2​ as negative in the composite formula.

  1. Remaining area:
A=A1−A2=9600−2800=6800 mm2.A=A_1-A_2=9600-2800=6800\ \text{mm}^2.A=A1​−A2​=9600−2800=6800 mm2.

The flange has a small local IxI_xIx​, but its distance from the centroid makes a large parallel-axis contribution.

Code​

parts = [
    # name, area, xbar, ybar
    ("large rectangle", 120.0 * 80.0, 60.0, 40.0),
    ("cutout", -70.0 * 40.0, 85.0, 60.0),
]

A = sum(area for name, area, x, y in parts)
xbar = sum(area * x for name, area, x, y in parts) / A
ybar = sum(area * y for name, area, x, y in parts) / A

print(f"area = {A:.1f} mm^2")
print(f"xbar = {xbar:.2f} mm")
print(f"ybar = {ybar:.2f} mm")

# Parallel-axis computation for the T-section.
rectangles = [
    ("web", 20.0, 120.0, 60.0),
    ("flange", 100.0, 20.0, 130.0),
]
area_total = sum(b * h for name, b, h, yc in rectangles)
y_centroid = sum(b * h * yc for name, b, h, yc in rectangles) / area_total
Ix = 0.0
for name, b, h, yc in rectangles:
    area = b * h
    Ix_local = b * h**3 / 12.0
    Ix += Ix_local + area * (yc - y_centroid) ** 2
print(f"T-section ybar = {y_centroid:.2f} mm, Ix = {Ix:.0f} mm^4")

Common pitfalls​

  • Treating a hole as a positive area in the composite centroid formula.
  • Using a second moment formula about the wrong axis or wrong centroidal orientation.
  • Applying the parallel-axis theorem with distance measured along the axis instead of perpendicular to it.
  • Confusing mass moment of inertia with second moment of area.
  • Dropping units: IxI_xIx​ for an area is length4^44, not length2^22.
  • Ignoring symmetry that could simplify a centroid or product-of-inertia calculation.
  • Rounding the centroid too early before using it in a parallel-axis term.
