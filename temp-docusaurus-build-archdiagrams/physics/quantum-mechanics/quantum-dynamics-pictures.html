Quantum Dynamics and Pictures

Quantum dynamics describes how probability amplitudes change between preparations and measurements. The same physics can be represented in several pictures: in the Schrodinger picture states move, in the Heisenberg picture operators move, and in the interaction picture both share the work in a way designed for perturbation theory.

Sakurai develops the time-evolution operator first, then compares pictures and applies the result to spin precession, Ehrenfest's theorem, and transition amplitudes. Ballentine puts dynamics inside a broader symmetry and transformation framework. The Gottfried-named notes emphasize propagators and density operators soon after the picture change. Schiff's wave-mechanics tradition corresponds most directly to Schrodinger's differential equation in coordinate representation.

Definitions​

The time-evolution operator satisfies

∣ψ(t)⟩=U(t,t0)∣ψ(t0)⟩.|\psi(t)\rangle=U(t,t_0)|\psi(t_0)\rangle.∣ψ(t)⟩=U(t,t0​)∣ψ(t0​)⟩.

For a closed system,

U†(t,t0)U(t,t0)=I.U^\dagger(t,t_0)U(t,t_0)=I.U†(t,t0​)U(t,t0​)=I.

If the Hamiltonian is time independent,

U(t,t0)=e−iH(t−t0)/ℏ.U(t,t_0)=e^{-iH(t-t_0)/\hbar}.U(t,t0​)=e−iH(t−t0​)/ℏ.

If H(t)H(t)H(t) depends on time and does not commute with itself at different times, the formal solution uses time ordering:

U(t,t0)=Texp⁡[−iℏ∫t0tH(t′) dt′].U(t,t_0)=\mathcal T\exp\left[-{i\over\hbar}\int_{t_0}^{t}H(t')\,dt'\right].U(t,t0​)=Texp[−ℏi​∫t0​t​H(t′)dt′].

In the Schrodinger picture, states carry time dependence:

iℏddt∣ψS(t)⟩=HS∣ψS(t)⟩,i\hbar {d\over dt}|\psi_S(t)\rangle=H_S|\psi_S(t)\rangle,iℏdtd​∣ψS​(t)⟩=HS​∣ψS​(t)⟩,

while operators are fixed unless they have explicit time dependence.

In the Heisenberg picture,

∣ψH⟩=∣ψS(t0)⟩,AH(t)=U†(t,t0)ASU(t,t0).|\psi_H\rangle=|\psi_S(t_0)\rangle, \qquad A_H(t)=U^\dagger(t,t_0)A_S U(t,t_0).∣ψH​⟩=∣ψS​(t0​)⟩,AH​(t)=U†(t,t0​)AS​U(t,t0​).

In the interaction picture, split H=H0+V(t)H=H_0+V(t)H=H0​+V(t) and define

∣ψI(t)⟩=eiH0(t−t0)/ℏ∣ψS(t)⟩,|\psi_I(t)\rangle=e^{iH_0(t-t_0)/\hbar}|\psi_S(t)\rangle,∣ψI​(t)⟩=eiH0​(t−t0​)/ℏ∣ψS​(t)⟩,

with

VI(t)=eiH0(t−t0)/ℏV(t)e−iH0(t−t0)/ℏ.V_I(t)=e^{iH_0(t-t_0)/\hbar}V(t)e^{-iH_0(t-t_0)/\hbar}.VI​(t)=eiH0​(t−t0​)/ℏV(t)e−iH0​(t−t0​)/ℏ.

Key results​

The Heisenberg equation of motion is

dAHdt=iℏ[HH,AH]+(∂AS∂t)H.{dA_H\over dt}={i\over\hbar}[H_H,A_H]+\left({\partial A_S\over\partial t}\right)_H.dtdAH​​=ℏi​[HH​,AH​]+(∂t∂AS​​)H​.

If AAA has no explicit time dependence and [A,H]=0[A,H]=0[A,H]=0, then AAA is conserved. This is the operator version of conservation laws, and it prepares the later symmetry page.

Expectation values are picture independent:

⟨ψS(t)∣AS∣ψS(t)⟩=⟨ψH∣AH(t)∣ψH⟩.\langle \psi_S(t)|A_S|\psi_S(t)\rangle =\langle \psi_H|A_H(t)|\psi_H\rangle.⟨ψS​(t)∣AS​∣ψS​(t)⟩=⟨ψH​∣AH​(t)∣ψH​⟩.

Ehrenfest's theorem follows by applying the Heisenberg equation to XXX and PPP for

H=P22m+V(X).H={P^2\over 2m}+V(X).H=2mP2​+V(X).

The result is

ddt⟨X⟩=⟨P⟩m,ddt⟨P⟩=−⟨dVdX⟩.{d\over dt}\langle X\rangle={\langle P\rangle\over m}, \qquad {d\over dt}\langle P\rangle=-\left\langle {dV\over dX}\right\rangle.dtd​⟨X⟩=m⟨P⟩​,dtd​⟨P⟩=−⟨dXdV​⟩.

This resembles Newton's law, but it is not identical unless the wave packet is narrow enough or the force is linear.

The interaction-picture state obeys

iℏddt∣ψI(t)⟩=VI(t)∣ψI(t)⟩.i\hbar {d\over dt}|\psi_I(t)\rangle=V_I(t)|\psi_I(t)\rangle.iℏdtd​∣ψI​(t)⟩=VI​(t)∣ψI​(t)⟩.

Iterating this integral equation gives the Dyson series:

∣ψI(t)⟩=∣ψI(t0)⟩−iℏ∫t0tdt1 VI(t1)∣ψI(t0)⟩+(−iℏ)2∫t0tdt1∫t0t1dt2 VI(t1)VI(t2)∣ψI(t0)⟩+⋯ .\begin{aligned} |\psi_I(t)\rangle &=|\psi_I(t_0)\rangle -{i\over\hbar}\int_{t_0}^{t}dt_1\,V_I(t_1)|\psi_I(t_0)\rangle\\ &\quad+\left(-{i\over\hbar}\right)^2 \int_{t_0}^{t}dt_1\int_{t_0}^{t_1}dt_2\,V_I(t_1)V_I(t_2)|\psi_I(t_0)\rangle+\cdots. \end{aligned}∣ψI​(t)⟩​=∣ψI​(t0​)⟩−ℏi​∫t0​t​dt1​VI​(t1​)∣ψI​(t0​)⟩+(−ℏi​)2∫t0​t​dt1​∫t0​t1​​dt2​VI​(t1​)VI​(t2​)∣ψI​(t0​)⟩+⋯.​

Sakurai uses this machinery for time-dependent perturbation theory. Ballentine's transformation-centered viewpoint makes clear that changing pictures is not changing the experiment; it is changing where time dependence is stored.

Worked example 1: Free particle Ehrenfest theorem​

Problem. For

H=P22m,H={P^2\over 2m},H=2mP2​,

show that the Heisenberg operator XH(t)X_H(t)XH​(t) moves linearly in time.

Method.

  1. Use the Heisenberg equation:
dXHdt=iℏ[H,XH].{dX_H\over dt}={i\over\hbar}[H,X_H].dtdXH​​=ℏi​[H,XH​].
  1. Compute the commutator using [X,P]=iℏ[X,P]=i\hbar[X,P]=iℏ or [P,X]=−iℏ[P,X]=-i\hbar[P,X]=−iℏ:
[P2,X]=P[P,X]+[P,X]P=−2iℏP.[P^2,X]=P[P,X]+[P,X]P=-2i\hbar P.[P2,X]=P[P,X]+[P,X]P=−2iℏP.
  1. Therefore
[H,X]=12m[P2,X]=−iℏmP.[H,X]={1\over 2m}[P^2,X]=-{i\hbar\over m}P.[H,X]=2m1​[P2,X]=−miℏ​P.
  1. Substitute:
dXHdt=iℏ(−iℏmPH)=PHm.{dX_H\over dt}={i\over\hbar}\left(-{i\hbar\over m}P_H\right)={P_H\over m}.dtdXH​​=ℏi​(−miℏ​PH​)=mPH​​.
  1. Since
dPHdt=iℏ[H,PH]=0,{dP_H\over dt}={i\over\hbar}[H,P_H]=0,dtdPH​​=ℏi​[H,PH​]=0,

integrate:

XH(t)=XH(0)+PH(0)mt.X_H(t)=X_H(0)+{P_H(0)\over m}t.XH​(t)=XH​(0)+mPH​(0)​t.

Checked answer. Taking expectation values gives ⟨X⟩(t)=⟨X⟩(0)+⟨P⟩t/m\langle X\rangle(t)=\langle X\rangle(0)+\langle P\rangle t/m⟨X⟩(t)=⟨X⟩(0)+⟨P⟩t/m, the quantum analogue of uniform classical motion.

Worked example 2: Two-level evolution under a diagonal Hamiltonian​

Problem. Let

H=ℏΩ2σz,∣ψ(0)⟩=12(∣+z⟩+∣−z⟩).H={\hbar\Omega\over 2}\sigma_z, \qquad |\psi(0)\rangle={1\over \sqrt2}(|+z\rangle+|-z\rangle).H=2ℏΩ​σz​,∣ψ(0)⟩=2​1​(∣+z⟩+∣−z⟩).

Find ∣ψ(t)⟩\vert \psi(t)\rangle∣ψ(t)⟩ and ⟨Sx⟩(t)\langle S_x\rangle(t)⟨Sx​⟩(t).

Method.

  1. The time-evolution operator is
U(t)=(e−iΩt/200eiΩt/2).U(t)=\begin{pmatrix}e^{-i\Omega t/2}&0\\0&e^{i\Omega t/2}\end{pmatrix}.U(t)=(e−iΩt/20​0eiΩt/2​).
  1. Apply it to the initial state:
∣ψ(t)⟩=12(e−iΩt/2∣+z⟩+eiΩt/2∣−z⟩).|\psi(t)\rangle={1\over \sqrt2}\left(e^{-i\Omega t/2}|+z\rangle+e^{i\Omega t/2}|-z\rangle\right).∣ψ(t)⟩=2​1​(e−iΩt/2∣+z⟩+eiΩt/2∣−z⟩).
  1. Use
Sx=ℏ2(0110).S_x={\hbar\over 2}\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}.Sx​=2ℏ​(01​10​).
  1. Compute the expectation:
⟨Sx⟩=ℏ2(12eiΩt+12e−iΩt)=ℏ2cos⁡(Ωt).\begin{aligned} \langle S_x\rangle &={\hbar\over 2}\left({1\over 2}e^{i\Omega t}+{1\over 2}e^{-i\Omega t}\right)\\ &={\hbar\over 2}\cos(\Omega t). \end{aligned}⟨Sx​⟩​=2ℏ​(21​eiΩt+21​e−iΩt)=2ℏ​cos(Ωt).​

Checked answer. The zzz probabilities remain 1/21/21/2 and 1/21/21/2, while the relative phase produces oscillation in the xxx expectation value.

Code​

import numpy as np

hbar = 1.0
omega = 2.0
sx = np.array([[0, 1], [1, 0]], dtype=complex) * hbar / 2
psi0 = np.array([1, 1], dtype=complex) / np.sqrt(2)

for t in np.linspace(0, np.pi / omega, 5):
    u = np.diag([np.exp(-1j * omega * t / 2), np.exp(1j * omega * t / 2)])
    psi = u @ psi0
    print(round(t, 3), np.vdot(psi, sx @ psi).real)

Common pitfalls​

  • Thinking the three pictures are different theories. They are equivalent representations of the same amplitudes.
  • Applying e−iHt/ℏe^{-iHt/\hbar}e−iHt/ℏ when H(t1)H(t_1)H(t1​) and H(t2)H(t_2)H(t2​) do not commute. Time ordering is then required.
  • Dropping explicit time dependence in the Heisenberg equation. The partial derivative term matters for driven observables.
  • Assuming Ehrenfest's theorem always reproduces one exact classical trajectory. It does so only in special limits.
  • Forgetting that interaction-picture operators evolve with H0H_0H0​, not the full Hamiltonian.
  • Confusing conservation of an operator with conservation of a particular measurement outcome. Conservation means the distribution is stationary for that observable under the stated Hamiltonian.
  • Losing unitarity in numerical time stepping. If a simulation changes the norm of a closed-system state, the integrator or approximation needs checking.

The most practical way to choose a picture is to identify what is simple. If the state has a simple initial expansion and the Hamiltonian is diagonal, the Schrodinger picture is usually direct. If the question asks how observables move or whether a quantity is conserved, the Heisenberg picture exposes the commutator structure. If the Hamiltonian splits into a solvable large part plus a weak time-dependent part, the interaction picture removes the trivial phases and leaves only transition-producing terms.

Picture independence is a useful error check. If a probability or expectation value is physical, it cannot depend on where the time dependence was placed. For example, a spin precession result computed by evolving the spinor must agree with the result computed by rotating the spin operator. If the answers differ, common causes include using UUU instead of U†U^\daggerU† in the Heisenberg operator, losing a sign in the exponent, or forgetting that bras evolve with the adjoint.

Time-dependent Hamiltonians require more care than the compact notation suggests. The expression e−i∫Hdt/ℏe^{-i\int Hdt/\hbar}e−i∫Hdt/ℏ is valid only when Hamiltonians at different times commute or when time ordering is included. This issue is not pedantic: driven two-level systems, magnetic resonance, and perturbation theory all involve operators whose values at different times may fail to commute. Sakurai's development of the time-evolution operator makes this point structurally clear.

Numerical dynamics should respect the same structure. Exact closed-system evolution is unitary, so a finite-step approximation that steadily changes the norm is not just imprecise; it is violating a core physical constraint. For small matrices, exponentiating a Hermitian Hamiltonian gives a unitary step. For differential wave equations, stable unitary or norm-preserving schemes are preferred when long-time phase accuracy matters.

A final check is to distinguish time dependence of phases from time dependence of probabilities. Energy eigenstates acquire phases, but their energy measurement probabilities do not change under a time-independent Hamiltonian. Superpositions acquire relative phases, and those relative phases can change probabilities for other observables. Many spin-precession and two-level interference effects are nothing more than this relative-phase evolution made visible.

When a problem includes measurement at an intermediate time, split the calculation into unitary pieces separated by state-update rules. Do not evolve through a measurement as if it were just another Hamiltonian interval unless the measurement interaction is explicitly modeled.
