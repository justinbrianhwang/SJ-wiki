Scalar Phi-Four Theory

Real scalar ϕ4\phi^4ϕ4 theory is the laboratory model of perturbative quantum field theory. It has a kinetic term, a mass term, and the simplest nontrivial local self-interaction in four spacetime dimensions. Because it avoids spin, gauge redundancy, and group theory, it lets the central machinery stand in the open: propagators, vertices, loops, divergences, counterterms, and running couplings.

The theory is also physically flexible. In relativistic particle physics it models contact scattering and spontaneous symmetry breaking. In condensed matter, its Euclidean version becomes the Landau-Ginzburg theory of critical phenomena. Zee uses this kind of model repeatedly because it is simple enough to calculate with and rich enough to display the logic of QFT.

Definitions​

The Lagrangian density for a real scalar with quartic interaction is

L=12∂μϕ ∂μϕ−12m2ϕ2−λ4!ϕ4.\mathcal{L}= \frac{1}{2}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi -\frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2 -\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4.L=21​∂μ​ϕ∂μϕ−21​m2ϕ2−4!λ​ϕ4.

The free propagator in momentum space is

Δ~F(p)=ip2−m2+iϵ.\tilde{\Delta}_F(p)=\frac{i}{p^2-m^2+i\epsilon}.Δ~F​(p)=p2−m2+iϵi​.

The four-point vertex is

−iλ.-i\lambda.−iλ.

The classical potential is

V(ϕ)=12m2ϕ2+λ4!ϕ4.V(\phi)=\frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2+\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4.V(ϕ)=21​m2ϕ2+4!λ​ϕ4.

For stability at large ∣ϕ∣\vert \phi\vert ∣ϕ∣, one wants λ>0\lambda\gt 0λ>0. If m2>0m^2\gt 0m2>0, the minimum is at ϕ=0\phi=0ϕ=0. If m2<0m^2\lt 0m2<0, the symmetric point becomes unstable and two nonzero minima appear, making the same model a prototype for spontaneous symmetry breaking.

The nnn-point correlation functions are generated from

Z[J]=∫Dϕ exp⁡(i∫d4x[L+Jϕ]).Z[J]= \int\mathcal{D}\phi\, \exp\left(i\int d^4x\left[ \mathcal{L}+J\phi \right]\right).Z[J]=∫Dϕexp(i∫d4x[L+Jϕ]).

Key results​

The field equation is nonlinear:

(∂2+m2)ϕ+λ3!ϕ3=0.(\partial^2+m^2)\phi+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^3=0.(∂2+m2)ϕ+3!λ​ϕ3=0.

Perturbatively, the first nontrivial correction to the two-point function is the tadpole loop. The first correction to the four-point amplitude is the bubble diagram. These two diagrams already show the two main renormalization targets: the mass and the coupling.

The superficial degree of divergence in four-dimensional ϕ4\phi^4ϕ4 theory is

D=4−E,D=4-E,D=4−E,

where EEE is the number of external legs. This follows from D=4L−2ID=4L-2ID=4L−2I and the graph identities for quartic vertices. It means that the two-point function is quadratically divergent by power counting, the four-point function logarithmically divergent, and higher-point functions superficially convergent. This is why only a finite list of counterterms is needed:

δL=12δZ ∂μϕ ∂μϕ−12δm2ϕ2−δλ4!ϕ4.\delta\mathcal{L}= \frac{1}{2}\delta Z\,\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi -\frac{1}{2}\delta m^2\phi^2 -\frac{\delta\lambda}{4!}\phi^4.δL=21​δZ∂μ​ϕ∂μϕ−21​δm2ϕ2−4!δλ​ϕ4.

At tree level the 2→22\to22→2 amplitude is constant, but loops introduce momentum dependence and scale dependence. The theory is therefore a compact testing ground for the idea that measured parameters depend on the scale at which they are measured.

The theory is also useful because the same Lagrangian supports several physical interpretations depending on parameters and signature. With m2>0m^2\gt 0m2>0 and Minkowski time, it describes a massive scalar particle with a short-range contact self-interaction. With m2<0m^2\lt 0m2<0, the classical potential has degenerate minima and becomes the simplest algebraic model of spontaneous symmetry breaking. After Wick rotation to Euclidean signature, the action resembles a statistical free energy for an order parameter, and the parameter m2m^2m2 plays the role of distance from a critical point.

Renormalization conditions can be stated directly in this model. One may demand that the exact two-point function has a pole at p2=mphys2p^2=m_{\text{phys}}^2p2=mphys2​ with residue one, and that the four-point amplitude at a specified symmetric momentum equals −λphys-\lambda_{\text{phys}}−λphys​. These requirements determine δm2\delta m^2δm2, δZ\delta ZδZ, and δλ\delta\lambdaδλ order by order. Other schemes choose different finite parts, but all schemes agree on physical observables after parameters are translated.

The model also illustrates why "simple" does not mean "trivial." In four dimensions the coupling is marginal at tree level, so loop effects decide its scale dependence. In dimensions below four, the quartic coupling becomes relevant and drives nontrivial critical behavior. In dimensions above four, it becomes irrelevant in the RG sense, and the free Gaussian fixed point dominates long-distance behavior unless other scales intervene.

Finally, ϕ4\phi^4ϕ4 theory is a warning about notation. The same symbol λ\lambdaλ may denote a bare coupling, a renormalized coupling at scale μ\muμ, or a measured low-energy parameter. A calculation is incomplete until it states which one is being used and how it is fixed.

Worked example 1: Classical minima and small oscillations​

Problem: Let

V(ϕ)=−12μ2ϕ2+λ4!ϕ4,μ2>0,λ>0.V(\phi)=-\frac{1}{2}\mu^2\phi^2+\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4, \qquad \mu^2>0,\quad \lambda>0.V(ϕ)=−21​μ2ϕ2+4!λ​ϕ4,μ2>0,λ>0.

Find the minima and the mass of small fluctuations about one minimum.

Step 1: Differentiate the potential:

dVdϕ=−μ2ϕ+λ3!ϕ3.\frac{dV}{d\phi}=-\mu^2\phi+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^3.dϕdV​=−μ2ϕ+3!λ​ϕ3.

Step 2: Set this equal to zero:

ϕ(−μ2+λ6ϕ2)=0.\phi\left(-\mu^2+\frac{\lambda}{6}\phi^2\right)=0.ϕ(−μ2+6λ​ϕ2)=0.

Step 3: The stationary points are

ϕ=0,ϕ2=6μ2λ.\phi=0, \qquad \phi^2=\frac{6\mu^2}{\lambda}.ϕ=0,ϕ2=λ6μ2​.

Define

v=6μ2λ.v=\sqrt{\frac{6\mu^2}{\lambda}}.v=λ6μ2​​.

Step 4: Use the second derivative:

d2Vdϕ2=−μ2+λ2ϕ2.\frac{d^2V}{d\phi^2}=-\mu^2+\frac{\lambda}{2}\phi^2.dϕ2d2V​=−μ2+2λ​ϕ2.

At ϕ=0\phi=0ϕ=0 this is −μ2-\mu^2−μ2, so the origin is unstable. At ϕ=±v\phi=\pm vϕ=±v,

d2Vdϕ2=−μ2+λ26μ2λ=2μ2.\frac{d^2V}{d\phi^2} =-\mu^2+\frac{\lambda}{2}\frac{6\mu^2}{\lambda} =2\mu^2.dϕ2d2V​=−μ2+2λ​λ6μ2​=2μ2.

Step 5: The small fluctuation ϕ=v+η\phi=v+\etaϕ=v+η has mass squared

mη2=2μ2.m_\eta^2=2\mu^2.mη2​=2μ2.

The checked result is two degenerate minima at ϕ=±v\phi=\pm vϕ=±v and a stable excitation of mass 2μ\sqrt{2}\mu2​μ around either one.

Worked example 2: Superficial divergence in phi-four theory​

Problem: Derive D=4−ED=4-ED=4−E for a connected ϕ4\phi^4ϕ4 graph in four spacetime dimensions.

Step 1: Each loop integral contributes four powers of momentum:

4L.4L.4L.

Step 2: Each internal scalar propagator behaves as 1/p21/p^21/p2 at large momentum, contributing

−2I.-2I.−2I.

Thus

D=4L−2I.D=4L-2I.D=4L−2I.

Step 3: For a connected graph,

L=I−V+1.L=I-V+1.L=I−V+1.

Substitute:

D=4(I−V+1)−2I=2I−4V+4.D=4(I-V+1)-2I=2I-4V+4.D=4(I−V+1)−2I=2I−4V+4.

Step 4: Count line ends:

4V=2I+E.4V=2I+E.4V=2I+E.

Therefore

2I=4V−E.2I=4V-E.2I=4V−E.

Step 5: Substitute:

D=(4V−E)−4V+4=4−E.D=(4V-E)-4V+4=4-E.D=(4V−E)−4V+4=4−E.

The checked answer is D=4−ED=4-ED=4−E. A two-point graph has D=2D=2D=2, a four-point graph has D=0D=0D=0, and a six-point graph has D=−2D=-2D=−2.

Code​

def superficial_degree_phi4(external_legs, spacetime_dim=4):
    if spacetime_dim != 4:
        raise ValueError("this shortcut is for four spacetime dimensions")
    return 4 - external_legs

for external in [2, 4, 6, 8]:
    degree = superficial_degree_phi4(external)
    verdict = "divergent" if degree >= 0 else "superficially convergent"
    print(f"E={external}: D={degree}, {verdict}")

Common pitfalls​

  • Forgetting the 4!4!4! in the interaction and then assigning the wrong vertex factor.
  • Treating λ>0\lambda\gt 0λ>0 as optional for a stable potential. Without higher stabilizing terms, negative λ\lambdaλ makes the potential unbounded.
  • Confusing the sign of m2m^2m2 in the Lagrangian with the sign in the potential.
  • Assuming every divergent graph requires a new physical parameter. Renormalizability means divergences can be absorbed into existing terms.
  • Ignoring disconnected vacuum bubbles when deriving diagrams; they cancel from normalized correlators but matter for vacuum energy discussions.
  • Using the same symbol for bare and renormalized quantities without saying which is meant. In loop calculations, λ0\lambda_0λ0​, λ(μ)\lambda(\mu)λ(μ), and a measured scattering coupling are different until a renormalization condition relates them.
  • Forgetting that the same polynomial potential has different physics in different regimes. Positive m2m^2m2 gives a symmetric massive phase; negative m2m^2m2 requires expanding around a nonzero vacuum; Euclidean signature turns the model into a critical-phenomena workhorse.
  • Treating power counting as the whole calculation. It predicts possible divergence, not the finite part, symmetry factor, threshold structure, or scheme-dependent subtraction.
  • Assuming the classical potential alone determines the quantum phase diagram. Loop corrections can shift masses, couplings, and the effective potential.
  • Expanding around an unstable point and then interpreting the resulting negative mass squared as a particle mass.

Connections​

Use this page as the controlled sandbox for the rest of the subject. Nearly every later complication has a scalar prototype: propagators come from the quadratic term, vertices from the quartic term, counterterms from divergent loops, symmetry breaking from the sign of m2m^2m2, and RG flow from the scale dependence of λ\lambdaλ. Once these mechanisms are clear in ϕ4\phi^4ϕ4 theory, spinors and gauge fields add structure without changing the basic perturbative logic.
