Vectors in Rn

Vectors in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn are ordered lists of real numbers, but they should be read as more than lists. They encode displacement, data, forces, coefficients, states, and solutions. Linear algebra studies operations that preserve the two essential vector actions: adding vectors and scaling them.

This page is the concrete starting point for the rest of the subject. Later pages abstract these same operations to general vector spaces, but the intuition usually comes from R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 and R3\mathbb{R}^3R3: arrows can be added tip-to-tail, stretched by scalars, and combined to reach new locations. The same algebra also describes columns of a matrix, feature vectors in data, and state vectors in applied models.

Definitions​

A vector in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn is

v=[v1v2⋮vn].\mathbf{v}= \begin{bmatrix} v_1\\ v_2\\ \vdots\\ v_n \end{bmatrix}.v=​v1​v2​⋮vn​​​.

Vector addition and scalar multiplication are componentwise:

u+v=[u1+v1⋮un+vn],cv=[cv1⋮cvn].\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}= \begin{bmatrix} u_1+v_1\\ \vdots\\ u_n+v_n \end{bmatrix}, \qquad c\mathbf{v}= \begin{bmatrix} cv_1\\ \vdots\\ cv_n \end{bmatrix}.u+v=​u1​+v1​⋮un​+vn​​​,cv=​cv1​⋮cvn​​​.

A linear combination of vectors v1,…,vk\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_kv1​,…,vk​ is

c1v1+⋯+ckvk.c_1\mathbf{v}_1+\cdots+c_k\mathbf{v}_k.c1​v1​+⋯+ck​vk​.

The span of a set of vectors is the set of all their linear combinations. In R2\mathbb{R}^2R2, one nonzero vector spans a line through the origin. Two nonparallel vectors span the whole plane.

The standard basis of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn is e1,…,en\mathbf{e}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{e}_ne1​,…,en​, where ei\mathbf{e}_iei​ has a 111 in position iii and zeros elsewhere. Every vector has coordinates relative to this basis:

v=v1e1+⋯+vnen.\mathbf{v}=v_1\mathbf{e}_1+\cdots+v_n\mathbf{e}_n.v=v1​e1​+⋯+vn​en​.

The dot product and norm are

u⋅v=u1v1+⋯+unvn,∥v∥=v⋅v.\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=u_1v_1+\cdots+u_nv_n, \qquad \|\mathbf{v}\|=\sqrt{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}}.u⋅v=u1​v1​+⋯+un​vn​,∥v∥=v⋅v​.

Key results​

The vector operations in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn satisfy the usual algebraic laws: addition is commutative and associative, there is a zero vector, every vector has an additive inverse, scalar multiplication distributes over vector addition, and scalar multiplication is compatible with multiplication of scalars.

Every vector in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn has a unique standard-basis expansion:

v=v1e1+⋯+vnen.\mathbf{v}=v_1\mathbf{e}_1+\cdots+v_n\mathbf{e}_n.v=v1​e1​+⋯+vn​en​.

Proof sketch: the right-hand side has iiith component viv_ivi​, so it equals v\mathbf{v}v. Uniqueness follows because equality of vectors means equality component by component.

The line through a point p\mathbf{p}p in direction v≠0\mathbf{v}\neq\mathbf{0}v=0 is

x=p+tv.\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{p}+t\mathbf{v}.x=p+tv.

The plane through p\mathbf{p}p in directions u,v\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}u,v is

x=p+su+tv,\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{p}+s\mathbf{u}+t\mathbf{v},x=p+su+tv,

provided u\mathbf{u}u and v\mathbf{v}v are not scalar multiples.

Linear dependence in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn means that at least one vector in a list can be built from the others. Equivalently, the equation

c1v1+⋯+ckvk=0c_1\mathbf{v}_1+\cdots+c_k\mathbf{v}_k=\mathbf{0}c1​v1​+⋯+ck​vk​=0

has a solution where not all cic_ici​ are zero. Dependence is a geometric warning that the listed directions contain redundancy.

Worked example 1: Combine vectors and check a span​

Problem: let

u=[2−13],v=[041].\mathbf{u}= \begin{bmatrix} 2\\-1\\3 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v}= \begin{bmatrix} 0\\4\\1 \end{bmatrix}.u=​2−13​​,v=​041​​.

Compute 3u−2v3\mathbf{u}-2\mathbf{v}3u−2v, and decide whether

w=[514]\mathbf{w}= \begin{bmatrix} 5\\1\\4 \end{bmatrix}w=​514​​

lies in span⁡{u,v}\operatorname{span}\{\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\}span{u,v}.

Step 1: compute the linear combination componentwise.

3u−2v=[6−39]−[082]=[6−117].3\mathbf{u}-2\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 6\\-3\\9 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 0\\8\\2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6\\-11\\7 \end{bmatrix}.3u−2v=​6−39​​−​082​​=​6−117​​.

Step 2: set up the span equation.

c1[2−13]+c2[041]=[514].c_1 \begin{bmatrix} 2\\-1\\3 \end{bmatrix} + c_2 \begin{bmatrix} 0\\4\\1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5\\1\\4 \end{bmatrix}.c1​​2−13​​+c2​​041​​=​514​​.

Step 3: compare components:

2c1=5,−c1+4c2=1,3c1+c2=4.2c_1=5, \qquad -c_1+4c_2=1, \qquad 3c_1+c_2=4.2c1​=5,−c1​+4c2​=1,3c1​+c2​=4.

The first equation gives c1=5/2c_1=5/2c1​=5/2. The second gives

−52+4c2=1⟹4c2=72⟹c2=78.-\frac52+4c_2=1 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 4c_2=\frac72 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad c_2=\frac78.−25​+4c2​=1⟹4c2​=27​⟹c2​=87​.

The third equation would require

3(52)+c2=4⟹c2=−72.3\left(\frac52\right)+c_2=4 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad c_2=-\frac72.3(25​)+c2​=4⟹c2​=−27​.

The two required values of c2c_2c2​ disagree. Checked answer: w\mathbf{w}w is not in the span of u\mathbf{u}u and v\mathbf{v}v.

Worked example 2: Parametrize a line and a plane​

Problem: find a parametric equation for the line through

p=[1−20]\mathbf{p}= \begin{bmatrix} 1\\-2\\0 \end{bmatrix}p=​1−20​​

in the direction

v=[31−4],\mathbf{v}= \begin{bmatrix} 3\\1\\-4 \end{bmatrix},v=​31−4​​,

then test whether q=[70−8]\mathbf{q}=\begin{bmatrix}7\\0\\-8\end{bmatrix}q=​70−8​​ lies on it.

Step 1: write the vector equation.

x=p+tv=[1−20]+t[31−4].\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{p}+t\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\-2\\0 \end{bmatrix} +t \begin{bmatrix} 3\\1\\-4 \end{bmatrix}.x=p+tv=​1−20​​+t​31−4​​.

Step 2: write component equations:

x=1+3t,y=−2+t,z=−4t.x=1+3t,\qquad y=-2+t,\qquad z=-4t.x=1+3t,y=−2+t,z=−4t.

Step 3: test q\mathbf{q}q. From x=7x=7x=7,

7=1+3t⟹t=2.7=1+3t \quad\Longrightarrow\quad t=2.7=1+3t⟹t=2.

With t=2t=2t=2, the yyy coordinate is −2+2=0-2+2=0−2+2=0, and the zzz coordinate is −8-8−8. Both match q\mathbf{q}q.

Checked answer: q\mathbf{q}q lies on the line, and it corresponds to t=2t=2t=2.

Code​

import numpy as np

u = np.array([2, -1, 3], dtype=float)
v = np.array([0, 4, 1], dtype=float)
w = np.array([5, 1, 4], dtype=float)

print(3 * u - 2 * v)

A = np.column_stack([u, v])
coeffs, residuals, rank, singular_values = np.linalg.lstsq(A, w, rcond=None)
print(coeffs)
print(A @ coeffs)
print(np.allclose(A @ coeffs, w))
print(rank, singular_values)

The least-squares call returns the closest vector in the span. allclose checks whether the match is exact up to floating-point tolerance; here it reports that the span equation is not exactly solvable.

Common pitfalls​

  • Treating vectors as points only. A vector can represent a point, displacement, data record, or coefficient list depending on context.
  • Adding vectors of different dimensions. Operations in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn require matching lengths.
  • Confusing span with a finite list of combinations. Span contains all scalar combinations, usually infinitely many.
  • Assuming two nonzero vectors in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 span all of R3\mathbb{R}^3R3. Two directions can span at most a plane.
  • Forgetting that the zero vector cannot determine a line direction by itself.
  • Deciding span membership by visual guess instead of solving the coefficient equation.

A strong habit is to translate every span question into a linear system. To ask whether w\mathbf{w}w lies in the span of v1,…,vk\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_kv1​,…,vk​, set

c1v1+⋯+ckvk=wc_1\mathbf{v}_1+\cdots+c_k\mathbf{v}_k=\mathbf{w}c1​v1​+⋯+ck​vk​=w

and solve for the coefficients. If the system is consistent, the vector is in the span. If it is inconsistent, the vector is outside the span. This method scales from pictures in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 to high-dimensional data where visualization is impossible.

Linear combinations also provide the first intuition for coordinates. In the standard basis, the coordinates of v\mathbf{v}v are its entries. In a nonstandard basis, coordinates are the weights needed to build the same vector from different basis directions. This distinction becomes essential when studying change of basis, diagonalization, and linear transformations.

The zero vector deserves special attention. It belongs to every span because all coefficients can be chosen as zero. It is also linearly dependent with any list that contains it, because 1⋅0=01\cdot\mathbf{0}=\mathbf{0}1⋅0=0 is a nontrivial relation. But it cannot serve as a direction vector for a line, since t0t\mathbf{0}t0 never moves away from the starting point.

In applications, vector entries should have a consistent interpretation. A vector might represent measurements, but adding two measurement vectors only makes sense if corresponding entries refer to the same quantities and units. Linear algebra supplies formal operations; modeling supplies the meaning of the components.

Vector equations are often cleaner than coordinate equations. The parametric line

x=p+tv\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{p}+t\mathbf{v}x=p+tv

encodes three component equations in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 and makes the direction visible. Similarly, a plane equation using two direction vectors shows immediately which movements stay inside the plane. Coordinate equations are useful for solving; vector equations are useful for structure.

The idea of a linear combination is the seed of matrix multiplication. If AAA has columns a1,…,an\mathbf{a}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{a}_na1​,…,an​, then

Ax=x1a1+⋯+xnan.A\mathbf{x}=x_1\mathbf{a}_1+\cdots+x_n\mathbf{a}_n.Ax=x1​a1​+⋯+xn​an​.

Thus every matrix-vector product is a column combination. This viewpoint makes systems, column spaces, rank, and linear transformations feel like continuations of vector arithmetic rather than separate topics.

Magnitude and direction should also be kept separate. Scaling by a positive scalar changes length but not direction; scaling by a negative scalar reverses direction; adding vectors changes both. Later, normalization will create unit vectors that preserve direction while simplifying length calculations.

A final useful distinction is between equality of vectors and equality of representations. In Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, equality means every corresponding component is equal. But the same geometric vector may be represented with different coordinates after a change of basis. The entries are not the essence of the vector; they are its coordinates in a chosen coordinate system.

Many later computations are just organized vector arithmetic. Solving a system asks whether one vector is a linear combination of others. Finding a basis asks for a nonredundant spanning list. Orthogonal projection asks for the closest vector inside a span. Keeping the basic vector operations clear makes those later topics much easier to connect.

Before moving on, be able to move between three descriptions of the same object: a column of entries, an arrow or displacement, and a linear combination of basis vectors. Most later errors come from forgetting which description is being used in a given argument.

Also practice checking dimensions aloud: only vectors in the same Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn can be added, and only matching component positions should be compared or combined.

That discipline prevents silent coordinate mistakes.
