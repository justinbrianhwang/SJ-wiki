Permutations and Combinations

Permutations and combinations count selections from a finite set. The key distinction is order. If the order of selected objects matters, use permutations or ordered arrangements. If order does not matter, use combinations.

These formulas are special cases of the product and division rules. The safest approach is to describe the object being counted in words before choosing a formula: ordered or unordered, with or without repetition, all objects or only some objects, distinct objects or repeated types.

Definitions​

A permutation of nnn distinct objects is an ordering of all nnn objects. There are

n!n!n!

permutations, where n!=n(n−1)⋯2⋅1n!=n(n-1)\cdots2\cdot1n!=n(n−1)⋯2⋅1 and 0!=10!=10!=1.

An rrr-permutation of nnn distinct objects is an ordered selection of rrr objects from nnn:

P(n,r)=n(n−1)⋯(n−r+1)=n!(n−r)!.P(n,r)=n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)=\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}.P(n,r)=n(n−1)⋯(n−r+1)=(n−r)!n!​.

An rrr-combination of nnn distinct objects is an unordered selection of rrr objects from nnn:

(nr)=n!r!(n−r)!.\binom{n}{r}=\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}.(rn​)=r!(n−r)!n!​.

A multiset permits repeated elements. Counting with repetition changes the formula. Ordered selections with repetition from nnn types have nrn^rnr possibilities. Unordered selections with repetition of rrr objects from nnn types have

(n+r−1r)\binom{n+r-1}{r}(rn+r−1​)

possibilities, the stars-and-bars count.

If a word has nnn letters with repeated multiplicities n1,n2,…,nkn_1,n_2,\dots,n_kn1​,n2​,…,nk​, then the number of distinct rearrangements is

n!n1!n2!⋯nk!.\frac{n!}{n_1!n_2!\cdots n_k!}.n1​!n2​!⋯nk​!n!​.

Key results​

The formula for combinations follows from the division rule. First count ordered selections:

P(n,r)=n!(n−r)!.P(n,r)=\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}.P(n,r)=(n−r)!n!​.

Each unordered rrr-element subset has exactly r!r!r! orderings, so

(nr)=P(n,r)r!=n!r!(n−r)!.\binom{n}{r}=\frac{P(n,r)}{r!}=\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}.(rn​)=r!P(n,r)​=r!(n−r)!n!​.

Symmetry:

(nr)=(nn−r).\binom{n}{r}=\binom{n}{n-r}.(rn​)=(n−rn​).

Choosing rrr objects to include is equivalent to choosing n−rn-rn−r objects to exclude.

Pascal's identity:

(nr)=(n−1r−1)+(n−1r).\binom{n}{r}=\binom{n-1}{r-1}+\binom{n-1}{r}.(rn​)=(r−1n−1​)+(rn−1​).

Proof: fix a particular element xxx. An rrr-subset either contains xxx, in which case choose r−1r-1r−1 more from the other n−1n-1n−1 elements, or does not contain xxx, in which case choose all rrr from the other n−1n-1n−1 elements.

The binomial theorem states

(x+y)n=∑r=0n(nr)xn−ryr.(x+y)^n=\sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}x^{n-r}y^r.(x+y)n=r=0∑n​(rn​)xn−ryr.

The coefficient (nr)\binom nr(rn​) counts the choice of which rrr of the nnn factors contribute a yyy term.

Stars and bars counts nonnegative integer solutions to

x1+x2+⋯+xn=rx_1+x_2+\cdots+x_n=rx1​+x2​+⋯+xn​=r

as (n+r−1r)\binom{n+r-1}{r}(rn+r−1​). Think of rrr stars and n−1n-1n−1 bars; the bars separate stars into nnn boxes.

Worked example 1: Count committees with roles​

Problem. From 121212 students, choose a committee of 444. Then choose a chair and secretary from the committee. How many outcomes are possible?

Method.

  1. First choose the committee. Order does not matter:
(124)=12!4!8!=495.\binom{12}{4}=\frac{12!}{4!8!}=495.(412​)=4!8!12!​=495.
  1. For each committee, choose the chair. There are 444 choices.
  2. Then choose the secretary from the remaining committee members. There are 333 choices.
  3. Multiply:
495⋅4⋅3=5940.495\cdot4\cdot3=5940.495⋅4⋅3=5940.
  1. Alternative check: choose the chair and secretary first as an ordered pair, then choose the other two committee members:
P(12,2)(102)=12⋅11⋅45=5940.P(12,2)\binom{10}{2}=12\cdot11\cdot45=5940.P(12,2)(210​)=12⋅11⋅45=5940.

Checked answer. There are 594059405940 outcomes. The two methods agree because they describe the same final object: a 444-person committee with two distinct roles assigned.

Worked example 2: Count solutions with stars and bars​

Problem. How many nonnegative integer solutions are there to

x1+x2+x3+x4=10?x_1+x_2+x_3+x_4=10?x1​+x2​+x3​+x4​=10?

Method.

  1. Interpret the equation as distributing 101010 identical objects among 444 boxes.
  2. Use 101010 stars and 4−1=34-1=34−1=3 bars.
  3. There are 10+3=1310+3=1310+3=13 positions in the star-bar string.
  4. Choose which 333 positions contain bars:
(133)=286.\binom{13}{3}=286.(313​)=286.
  1. Equivalently choose the 101010 positions containing stars:
(1310)=286.\binom{13}{10}=286.(1013​)=286.

Checked answer. There are 286286286 nonnegative integer solutions. For example, the pattern **|*****||*** corresponds to (2,5,0,3)(2,5,0,3)(2,5,0,3).

Code​

from math import factorial, comb, perm

def multiset_permutations(counts):
    total = sum(counts)
    result = factorial(total)
    for c in counts:
        result //= factorial(c)
    return result

def stars_and_bars(types, items):
    return comb(types + items - 1, items)

print(factorial(5))
print(perm(10, 3))
print(comb(52, 5))
print(multiset_permutations([1, 4, 4, 2]))  # MISSISSIPPI
print(stars_and_bars(4, 10))

Python's math.comb and math.perm are exact integer functions, useful for checking combinatorial calculations.

Common pitfalls​

  • Using permutations when order does not matter.
  • Dividing by r!r!r! when repetition or repeated types mean not every object has exactly r!r!r! orderings.
  • Forgetting that 0!=10!=10!=1, which is needed for edge cases such as choosing all objects.
  • Applying stars and bars to distinct objects. It counts identical objects distributed among labeled boxes.
  • Counting roles and membership in the wrong order without checking that the final object is counted exactly once.
  • Treating (nr)\binom nr(rn​) as zero or undefined without checking conventions; for integer counting, it is 000 when r<0r\lt 0r<0 or r>nr\gt nr>n.

When a problem mixes membership and roles, decide what the final outcome contains. A committee with a chair is not merely an unordered set; it is an unordered set plus one distinguished member. You can count by choosing the committee first and then the chair, or by choosing the chair first and then the remaining members. Both methods should agree if they describe each final outcome exactly once. If they do not agree, one method is probably counting a different object.

Repeated letters and repeated selections are different issues. The word LEVEL has repeated letters already present, so distinct rearrangements require dividing by repeated multiplicities. By contrast, forming a length-555 password from letters with repetition allowed means each position may independently choose any letter. That count is 26526^5265, not 26!/21!26!/21!26!/21! and not a multiset count, because the positions are ordered.

Stars and bars also has boundary conditions. It counts nonnegative solutions by default. If a problem requires positive solutions to x1+⋯+xk=nx_1+\cdots+x_k=nx1​+⋯+xk​=n, first set yi=xi−1y_i=x_i-1yi​=xi​−1, so the transformed equation is y1+⋯+yk=n−ky_1+\cdots+y_k=n-ky1​+⋯+yk​=n−k with yi≥0y_i\ge0yi​≥0. If upper bounds appear, plain stars and bars is no longer enough; use inclusion-exclusion or generating functions to remove solutions violating the bounds.

Binomial coefficients have several interchangeable interpretations. (nr)\binom nr(rn​) can count subsets of size rrr, paths with rrr steps of one kind and n−rn-rn−r of another, coefficients in (1+x)n(1+x)^n(1+x)n, or ways to place rrr identical markers among nnn labeled positions. Choosing the interpretation that matches the problem often gives a proof as well as a number.

A strong final check is to compare extreme cases. If r=0r=0r=0, there should usually be one way to choose nothing. If r=nr=nr=n, there should usually be one way to choose everything. If a formula fails these edge cases, the model likely used ordered choices, repetition, or division incorrectly.

For arrangements around a circle, be careful about symmetries. Seating nnn distinct people around a round table usually gives (n−1)!(n-1)!(n−1)! arrangements because rotating the whole table does not change the seating. If mirror images are also considered identical, divide by another factor of 222 for n>2n\gt 2n>2. These formulas are not ordinary permutations; they come from deciding which transformations leave the final arrangement unchanged.

For distributions, distinguish labeled and unlabeled boxes. Stars and bars counts identical objects into labeled boxes: giving 333 candies to Alice and 222 to Bo is different from giving 222 to Alice and 333 to Bo. If boxes are also unlabeled, the problem becomes an integer partition problem and requires different tools. Many mistakes come from silently changing whether recipients are distinct.

When a problem says "at least" or "at most," formulas often need summation. Choosing at most 333 members from 101010 is not (103)\binom{10}{3}(310​); it is (100)+(101)+(102)+(103)\binom{10}{0}+\binom{10}{1}+\binom{10}{2}+\binom{10}{3}(010​)+(110​)+(210​)+(310​). Choosing at least 111 may be easier by complement: 210−12^{10}-1210−1 nonempty subsets of a 101010-element set.

A useful way to avoid formula hunting is to build the object in stages. If the stages are ordered choices, multiply. If the staged construction imposes an artificial order that the final object does not have, divide by the number of artificial orderings. This explains the formulas instead of treating them as separate memorized facts.

For combinations with restrictions, consider whether to choose the special objects first or last. Counting poker hands with exactly two aces starts by choosing the aces and then choosing non-aces. Counting committees with at least one senior may be easier by complement. The right order is the one that makes later choices independent and avoids overlap.

If a counting problem has identical objects and labeled boxes, stars and bars may apply. If the objects are distinct and the boxes are labeled, each object chooses a box, giving a product count instead. If both objects and boxes are unlabeled, the problem is usually about partitions and needs different methods.
