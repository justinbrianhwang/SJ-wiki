Common Continuous Distributions

Continuous distributions model measurements that vary on intervals: times, lengths, errors, proportions, variances, and transformed statistics. They replace probability at a point with probability over intervals. For example, the probability that a lifetime is exactly 5.0000005.0000005.000000 years is usually zero, but the probability that it lies between 555 and 666 years can be positive.

Lane et al.'s statistics text treats the normal distribution in depth and later introduces distributions such as ttt and chi-square for inference. This probability page gives a theory-focused reference to the main continuous families, while cross-linking to statistics where the inferential use is the main topic.

Memorylessness of exponential. If X∼Exponential⁡(λ)X\sim\operatorname{Exponential}(\lambda)X∼Exponential(λ), then

P(X>s+t∣X>s)=P(X>t).P(X>s+t\mid X>s)=P(X>t).P(X>s+t∣X>s)=P(X>t).

The exponential is the continuous analogue of the geometric distribution.

Normal standardization. If X∼N(μ,σ2)X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)X∼N(μ,σ2), then

Z=X−μσ∼N(0,1).Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\sim N(0,1).Z=σX−μ​∼N(0,1).

Thus normal probabilities can be reduced to the standard normal CDF Φ\PhiΦ.

Gamma sums. If independent exponential waiting times share rate λ\lambdaλ, then their sum has a Gamma distribution. Specifically, the waiting time until the α\alphaα-th event in a Poisson process has Gamma(α,λ)(\alpha,\lambda)(α,λ) distribution when α\alphaα is a positive integer.

Several continuous families are connected by transformations. If Z∼N(0,1)Z\sim N(0,1)Z∼N(0,1), then Z2∼χ12Z^2\sim\chi^2_1Z2∼χ12​, and sums of independent squared standard normals produce chi-square distributions with more degrees of freedom. Ratios involving a standard normal and an independent chi-square variable produce ttt distributions. Ratios of scaled independent chi-square variables produce FFF distributions. These relationships explain why the same families appear repeatedly in sampling theory.

Location and scale also matter. If ZZZ has a standard distribution, then X=μ+σZX=\mu+\sigma ZX=μ+σZ shifts the center by μ\muμ and stretches the spread by σ\sigmaσ. Normal distributions are closed under this transformation, but many positive distributions are better described by shape and rate or shape and scale. Always record the parameterization. For Gamma and Exponential distributions, "rate" λ\lambdaλ and "scale" θ=1/λ\theta=1/\lambdaθ=1/λ are reciprocals, and confusing them changes the mean.

For reliability and survival problems, the survival function S(x)=P(X>x)S(x)=P(X\gt x)S(x)=P(X>x) is often clearer than the CDF. The exponential survival function S(x)=e−λxS(x)=e^{-\lambda x}S(x)=e−λx makes the constant-hazard assumption visible.

Continuous families are often chosen for shape constraints. Beta distributions can be U-shaped, uniform, left-skewed, right-skewed, or concentrated near the center depending on α\alphaα and β\betaβ. Gamma distributions can be highly right-skewed for small shape and more symmetric for large shape. Normal distributions are symmetric and light-tailed. These shape facts should be checked against context before fitting a model or using a table.

Quantiles are often more stable to communicate than densities. For example, saying that 95%95\%95% of a normal distribution lies within about two standard deviations of the mean is more interpretable than quoting density heights. For skewed distributions, report asymmetric intervals rather than forcing a mean-plus-minus-standard-deviation summary.

Worked example 1: exponential reliability​

Problem. A component lifetime XXX is exponential with mean 200200200 hours. Find the rate λ\lambdaλ, the probability it lasts at least 300300300 hours, and the probability it lasts another 100100100 hours given that it has already lasted 300300300 hours.

Method.

  1. For an exponential distribution, E[X]=1/λE[X]=1/\lambdaE[X]=1/λ. Since the mean is 200200200,
λ=1200=0.005.\lambda=\frac{1}{200}=0.005.λ=2001​=0.005.
  1. The survival function is
P(X>x)=e−λx.P(X>x)=e^{-\lambda x}.P(X>x)=e−λx.
  1. Probability of lasting at least 300300300 hours:
P(X≥300)=e−0.005(300)=e−1.5≈0.2231.P(X\ge 300)=e^{-0.005(300)}=e^{-1.5}\approx 0.2231.P(X≥300)=e−0.005(300)=e−1.5≈0.2231.
  1. Conditional probability of lasting another 100100100 hours after 300300300:
P(X>400∣X>300)=P(X>400)P(X>300).P(X>400\mid X>300)=\frac{P(X>400)}{P(X>300)}.P(X>400∣X>300)=P(X>300)P(X>400)​.
  1. Substitute survival probabilities:
e−0.005(400)e−0.005(300)=e−0.005(100)=e−0.5≈0.6065.\begin{aligned} \frac{e^{-0.005(400)}}{e^{-0.005(300)}} &=e^{-0.005(100)}\\ &=e^{-0.5}\\ &\approx 0.6065. \end{aligned}e−0.005(300)e−0.005(400)​​=e−0.005(100)=e−0.5≈0.6065.​

Worked example 2: normal probability by standardization​

Problem. Exam scores are approximately normal with mean 707070 and standard deviation 888. What proportion of scores lies between 626262 and 848484?

Method.

  1. Let X∼N(70,82)X\sim N(70,8^2)X∼N(70,82).

  2. Standardize the lower endpoint:

z1=62−708=−1.z_1=\frac{62-70}{8}=-1.z1​=862−70​=−1.
  1. Standardize the upper endpoint:
z2=84−708=1.75.z_2=\frac{84-70}{8}=1.75.z2​=884−70​=1.75.
  1. Convert to standard normal probability:
P(62≤X≤84)=P(−1≤Z≤1.75).P(62\le X\le 84)=P(-1\le Z\le 1.75).P(62≤X≤84)=P(−1≤Z≤1.75).
  1. Use the standard normal CDF:
P(−1≤Z≤1.75)=Φ(1.75)−Φ(−1).P(-1\le Z\le 1.75)=\Phi(1.75)-\Phi(-1).P(−1≤Z≤1.75)=Φ(1.75)−Φ(−1).
  1. With Φ(1.75)≈0.9599\Phi(1.75)\approx 0.9599Φ(1.75)≈0.9599 and Φ(−1)≈0.1587\Phi(-1)\approx 0.1587Φ(−1)≈0.1587,
P(62≤X≤84)≈0.9599−0.1587=0.8012.P(62\le X\le 84)\approx 0.9599-0.1587=0.8012.P(62≤X≤84)≈0.9599−0.1587=0.8012.

Code​

from scipy.stats import expon, norm, gamma, beta, chi2, t, f

# Exponential reliability.
mean_life = 200
rate = 1 / mean_life
survive_300 = expon.sf(300, scale=1 / rate)
survive_another_100 = expon.sf(100, scale=1 / rate)
print(rate, survive_300, survive_another_100)

# Normal interval probability.
mu, sigma = 70, 8
prob = norm.cdf(84, loc=mu, scale=sigma) - norm.cdf(62, loc=mu, scale=sigma)
print(prob)

# A small reference table.
print("Gamma mean:", gamma.mean(a=3, scale=1 / 2))  # rate 2 means scale 1/2
print("Beta mean:", beta.mean(a=2, b=5))
print("Chi-square 95th percentile:", chi2.ppf(0.95, df=10))
print("t 97.5th percentile:", t.ppf(0.975, df=12))
print("F 95th percentile:", f.ppf(0.95, dfn=5, dfd=20))

Common pitfalls​

  • Confusing rate and scale. SciPy often uses scale = 1 / rate, while many textbooks write exponential and gamma distributions with rate λ\lambdaλ.
  • Treating density height as probability. For continuous variables, probabilities are areas.
  • Assuming all bell-shaped data are normal. Tail behavior and skew matter.
  • Applying ttt, chi-square, or FFF formulas without their normal-sample assumptions when doing inference.
  • Forgetting support. A normal model can assign small probability to impossible negative values; sometimes this is acceptable, sometimes not.
  • Using the exponential memoryless property for non-exponential lifetimes. Aging components often do not have constant hazard.
