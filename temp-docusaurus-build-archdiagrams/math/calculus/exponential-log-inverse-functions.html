Exponential Log and Inverse Functions

Exponential, logarithmic, and inverse functions are where calculus moves beyond polynomial behavior. Exponentials model quantities whose rate of change is proportional to the current amount. Logarithms undo exponential growth and convert multiplication into addition. Inverse functions reverse input-output roles, so their derivatives explain how rates change when a relationship is solved in the opposite direction.

These functions appear in growth and decay, compound interest, radioactive half-life, pH, sound intensity, population models, and change of scale. They also support many integration techniques and differential equations later in calculus.

Definitions​

The natural exponential function exe^xex is the unique function satisfying

ddxex=ex,e0=1.\frac{d}{dx}e^x=e^x, \qquad e^0=1.dxd​ex=ex,e0=1.

For a positive base a≠1a\ne 1a=1,

ax=exln⁡a.a^x=e^{x\ln a}.ax=exlna.

The natural logarithm ln⁡x\ln xlnx is the inverse of exe^xex:

y=ln⁡x⟺ey=x,x>0.y=\ln x \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad e^y=x, \qquad x>0.y=lnx⟺ey=x,x>0.

Logarithm laws follow from exponent laws:

ln⁡(ab)=ln⁡a+ln⁡b,ln⁡(ab)=ln⁡a−ln⁡b,ln⁡(ar)=rln⁡a.\begin{aligned} \ln(ab)&=\ln a+\ln b,\\ \ln\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)&=\ln a-\ln b,\\ \ln(a^r)&=r\ln a. \end{aligned}ln(ab)ln(ba​)ln(ar)​=lna+lnb,=lna−lnb,=rlna.​

An inverse function reverses a one-to-one function. If f(a)=bf(a)=bf(a)=b, then f−1(b)=af^{-1}(b)=af−1(b)=a. The graphs of fff and f−1f^{-1}f−1 are reflections across the line y=xy=xy=x. A function is one-to-one if no horizontal line meets its graph more than once.

For inverse trigonometric functions, the original trigonometric functions must be restricted to intervals where they are one-to-one. For example, arcsin⁡x\arcsin xarcsinx is the inverse of sin⁡x\sin xsinx restricted to [−π/2,π/2][-\pi/2,\pi/2][−π/2,π/2].

Exponential growth and decay have the form

P(t)=P0ekt.P(t)=P_0e^{kt}.P(t)=P0​ekt.

Logarithmic differentiation is useful for products, quotients, and variable exponents. If y=xxy=x^xy=xx for x>0x\gt 0x>0, then ln⁡y=xln⁡x\ln y=x\ln xlny=xlnx, so

Inverse trigonometric derivative formulas include

ddxarcsin⁡x=11−x2,ddxarctan⁡x=11+x2.\frac{d}{dx}\arcsin x=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\arctan x=\frac{1}{1+x^2}.dxd​arcsinx=1−x2​1​,dxd​arctanx=1+x21​.

The domains are part of the result. The derivative of arcsin⁡x\arcsin xarcsinx is real only for −1<x<1-1\lt x\lt 1−1<x<1.

The change-of-base formula is often the simplest way to move between logarithms:

log⁡ax=ln⁡xln⁡a.\log_a x=\frac{\ln x}{\ln a}.loga​x=lnalnx​.

Since ln⁡a\ln alna is a constant, differentiating gives ddxlog⁡ax=1/(xln⁡a)\frac{d}{dx}\log_a x=1/(x\ln a)dxd​loga​x=1/(xlna). This is also why natural logarithms dominate calculus: base eee removes the extra constant from the derivative.

Logarithmic scales compress multiplicative change. If a quantity is multiplied by 101010, its common logarithm increases by 111. If a quantity is multiplied by eee, its natural logarithm increases by 111. This is why logarithms are used for pH, decibels, earthquake magnitude, and data spanning many orders of magnitude. Calculus on a logarithmic scale often turns relative change into ordinary change:

ddtln⁡P(t)=P′(t)P(t).\frac{d}{dt}\ln P(t)=\frac{P'(t)}{P(t)}.dtd​lnP(t)=P(t)P′(t)​.

The expression P′(t)/P(t)P'(t)/P(t)P′(t)/P(t) is the relative growth rate. If it is constant, the model is exponential. If it varies with time, the model may still be analyzed by integrating the relative growth rate.

Inverse-function derivatives have a geometric meaning. If the graph of fff has a steep tangent at (a,b)(a,b)(a,b), then the reflected graph of f−1f^{-1}f−1 has a shallow tangent at (b,a)(b,a)(b,a). Slopes become reciprocals because reflection across y=xy=xy=x swaps horizontal and vertical changes. This interpretation also explains why f′(a)=0f'(a)=0f′(a)=0 creates trouble: the reciprocal slope would be infinite, so the inverse has a vertical tangent or fails to be a differentiable function there.

In applications, exponential models should be checked against assumptions. Continuous compounding assumes growth occurs at every instant, not only once per year. Radioactive decay uses exponential behavior because each atom has a constant probability of decay per unit time. A population may grow approximately exponentially for a while, but limited resources eventually make a logistic or other constrained model more realistic.

Solving exponential equations usually means isolating the exponential expression and then taking logarithms. Solving logarithmic equations usually means combining logarithms carefully and then exponentiating. Each step must preserve the domain. For example, an equation involving ln⁡(x−3)\ln(x-3)ln(x−3) automatically requires x>3x\gt 3x>3, and any solution outside that domain must be rejected even if it appears after algebraic manipulation.

The constant eee also appears as a limiting growth factor:

e=lim⁡n→∞(1+1n)n.e=\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1+\frac1n\right)^n.e=n→∞lim​(1+n1​)n.

This limit connects continuous growth with increasingly frequent compounding. If interest at annual rate rrr is compounded nnn times per year, the growth factor over one year is

(1+rn)n.\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^n.(1+nr​)n.

As nnn increases without bound, the factor approaches ere^rer. This is why erte^{rt}ert is the natural continuous compounding model.

For inverse trigonometric functions, remember that the output is an angle in a restricted interval. The statement arcsin⁡(1/2)=π/6\arcsin(1/2)=\pi/6arcsin(1/2)=π/6 is not listing every angle whose sine is 1/21/21/2; it gives the principal value in [−π/2,π/2][-\pi/2,\pi/2][−π/2,π/2]. Calculus formulas for inverse trigonometric derivatives rely on those principal branches.

Growth comparisons are another reason these functions are central. For large positive xxx, exponential functions eventually outgrow every power xnx^nxn, while logarithms grow more slowly than every positive power xpx^pxp. These facts are often summarized by limits such as

lim⁡x→∞xnex=0,lim⁡x→∞ln⁡xxp=0\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^n}{e^x}=0, \qquad \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{\ln x}{x^p}=0x→∞lim​exxn​=0,x→∞lim​xplnx​=0

for fixed n>0n\gt 0n>0 and p>0p\gt 0p>0. They help estimate series, improper integrals, and algorithms.

They also explain why graph scales matter: exponential curves may look flat and then suddenly steep on ordinary axes, while logarithmic axes reveal multiplicative patterns more evenly across large ranges of data.

This matters in modeling, estimation, and asymptotic comparison.

Worked example 1: differentiate exponential and logarithmic expressions​

Problem. Differentiate

y=e3x2−1ln⁡(5x+2)y=e^{3x^2-1}\ln(5x+2)y=e3x2−1ln(5x+2)

Method.

  1. Find the domain. The exponential is defined for all real xxx, but the logarithm requires
5x+2>0⇒x>−25.5x+2>0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad x>-\frac25.5x+2>0⇒x>−52​.

Code​

from math import exp, log

def decay_model(initial_mass, half_life, t):
    k = -log(2) / half_life
    return initial_mass * exp(k * t)

for years in [0, 12, 24, 30]:
    print(years, round(decay_model(80, 12, years), 4))

Common pitfalls​

  • Writing ln⁡(a+b)=ln⁡a+ln⁡b\ln(a+b)=\ln a+\ln bln(a+b)=lna+lnb. Logarithms turn products into sums, not sums into sums.
  • Forgetting domain restrictions such as x>0x\gt 0x>0 for ln⁡x\ln xlnx.
  • Treating f−1(x)f^{-1}(x)f−1(x) as 1/f(x)1/f(x)1/f(x). Inverse function notation is not reciprocal notation.
  • Using the inverse derivative formula when f′(a)=0f'(a)=0f′(a)=0. The inverse may have a vertical tangent or fail to be differentiable.
  • Dropping the chain-rule factor in derivatives such as ln⁡(5x+2)\ln(5x+2)ln(5x+2) or e3x2e^{3x^2}e3x2.
  • Confusing half-life with the decay constant. The decay constant is k=−ln⁡2/Tk=-\ln 2/Tk=−ln2/T, not TTT itself.
