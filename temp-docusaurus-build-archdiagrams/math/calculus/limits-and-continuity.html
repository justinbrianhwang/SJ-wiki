Limits and Continuity

A limit describes the value a function approaches, not necessarily the value it actually attains. This distinction lets calculus handle holes, jumps, asymptotes, oscillations, and functions whose formulas change from one region to another. Continuity is the special case where approaching and evaluating agree.

Limits sit between algebra and the derivative. The derivative is defined as a limit of slopes, and the definite integral is defined as a limit of sums. A weak understanding of limits usually becomes a weak understanding of every later topic, so the goal is not just to compute limits but to recognize what kind of behavior the graph has near the point.

Definitions​

We write

lim⁡x→af(x)=L\lim_{x\to a} f(x)=Lx→alim​f(x)=L

when f(x)f(x)f(x) can be made arbitrarily close to LLL by taking xxx sufficiently close to aaa, with x≠ax\ne ax=a. The value f(a)f(a)f(a) is irrelevant to the limit except in the special case of continuity.

The one-sided limits are

lim⁡x→a−f(x),lim⁡x→a+f(x).\lim_{x\to a^-} f(x),\qquad \lim_{x\to a^+} f(x).x→a−lim​f(x),x→a+lim​f(x).

The two-sided limit exists exactly when both one-sided limits exist and are equal:

lim⁡x→a−f(x)=lim⁡x→a+f(x)=L.\lim_{x\to a^-} f(x)=\lim_{x\to a^+} f(x)=L.x→a−lim​f(x)=x→a+lim​f(x)=L.

A function is continuous at aaa when all three conditions hold:

f(a) is defined,lim⁡x→af(x) exists,lim⁡x→af(x)=f(a).\begin{aligned} f(a) &\text{ is defined},\\ \lim_{x\to a} f(x) &\text{ exists},\\ \lim_{x\to a} f(x) &= f(a). \end{aligned}f(a)x→alim​f(x)x→alim​f(x)​ is defined, exists,=f(a).​

An infinite limit, such as lim⁡x→a+f(x)=∞\lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)=\inftylimx→a+​f(x)=∞, describes unbounded behavior rather than a real-number limit. A vertical asymptote occurs when a one-sided limit is infinite. A horizontal asymptote occurs when

lim⁡x→∞f(x)=Lorlim⁡x→−∞f(x)=L.\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=L \quad\text{or}\quad \lim_{x\to-\infty} f(x)=L.x→∞lim​f(x)=Lorx→−∞lim​f(x)=L.

The ϵ\epsilonϵ-δ\deltaδ definition gives the formal meaning: lim⁡x→af(x)=L\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=Llimx→a​f(x)=L if for every ϵ>0\epsilon\gt 0ϵ>0 there exists δ>0\delta\gt 0δ>0 such that

0<∣x−a∣<δ⇒∣f(x)−L∣<ϵ.0<|x-a|<\delta \quad\Rightarrow\quad |f(x)-L|<\epsilon.0<∣x−a∣<δ⇒∣f(x)−L∣<ϵ.

The phrase "deleted window" is important. The condition 0<∣x−a∣0\lt \vert x-a\vert 0<∣x−a∣ excludes the point aaa itself. A function may be undefined at aaa, or may be assigned a value far from LLL, while still having limit LLL. Continuity adds the missing requirement that the function value and limiting value match.

Continuity on an interval means continuity at every point inside the interval and one-sided continuity at endpoints. On [a,b][a,b][a,b], continuity at aaa uses the right-hand limit, and continuity at bbb uses the left-hand limit. This convention matters in optimization and integration, where closed intervals include endpoints but the function may not be defined beyond them.

Key results​

The standard limit laws allow sums, differences, products, quotients, powers, and roots to be moved through limits when the resulting expressions are defined. If lim⁡f(x)=A\lim f(x)=Alimf(x)=A and lim⁡g(x)=B\lim g(x)=Blimg(x)=B as x→ax\to ax→a, then

lim⁡(f+g)=A+B,lim⁡(fg)=AB,lim⁡fg=AB(B≠0).\begin{aligned} \lim(f+g) &= A+B,\\ \lim(fg) &= AB,\\ \lim\frac{f}{g} &= \frac{A}{B}\quad (B\ne 0). \end{aligned}lim(f+g)lim(fg)limgf​​=A+B,=AB,=BA​(B=0).​

For polynomials, substitution works:

lim⁡x→ap(x)=p(a).\lim_{x\to a}p(x)=p(a).x→alim​p(x)=p(a).

For rational functions, if q(a)≠0q(a)\ne 0q(a)=0, then

lim⁡x→ap(x)q(x)=p(a)q(a).\lim_{x\to a}\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}=\frac{p(a)}{q(a)}.x→alim​q(x)p(x)​=q(a)p(a)​.

If substitution gives 0/00/00/0, the expression is indeterminate, not impossible. Algebra may reveal a removable discontinuity:

x2−a2x−a=x+a(x≠a).\frac{x^2-a^2}{x-a}=x+a\quad (x\ne a).x−ax2−a2​=x+a(x=a).

The simplified expression has the same values near aaa but not necessarily at aaa. This distinction is why cancellation is valid for limits but not for redefining the original function without saying so.

The Squeeze Theorem states that if g(x)≤f(x)≤h(x)g(x)\le f(x)\le h(x)g(x)≤f(x)≤h(x) near aaa and

lim⁡x→ag(x)=lim⁡x→ah(x)=L,\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=\lim_{x\to a}h(x)=L,x→alim​g(x)=x→alim​h(x)=L,

then lim⁡x→af(x)=L\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=Llimx→a​f(x)=L. It is especially useful for oscillating functions such as xsin⁡(1/x)x\sin(1/x)xsin(1/x).

The Intermediate Value Theorem is a central consequence of continuity. If fff is continuous on [a,b][a,b][a,b] and NNN lies between f(a)f(a)f(a) and f(b)f(b)f(b), then there is at least one ccc in [a,b][a,b][a,b] such that f(c)=Nf(c)=Nf(c)=N. The theorem does not say where ccc is or how many such points exist. It says that a continuous graph cannot jump over an intermediate height.

The Extreme Value Theorem is another consequence used later. If fff is continuous on a closed interval [a,b][a,b][a,b], then fff attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on that interval. The hypotheses are both necessary: an open interval may fail to include the height it approaches, and a discontinuous function may have a missing or unbounded value.

For rational limits at infinity, divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of xxx in the denominator. Lower powers vanish because

lim⁡x→∞1xk=0(k>0).\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac1{x^k}=0\quad (k>0).x→∞lim​xk1​=0(k>0).

For limits involving absolute value, roots, or piecewise definitions, one-sided analysis is often cleaner than forcing a single formula. For example, ∣x∣/x\vert x\vert /x∣x∣/x equals −1-1−1 for x<0x\lt 0x<0 and 111 for x>0x\gt 0x>0, so the two-sided limit at 000 does not exist. For square roots, the domain itself may force a one-sided limit, such as lim⁡x→0+x=0\lim_{x\to 0^+}\sqrt{x}=0limx→0+​x​=0.

A short ϵ\epsilonϵ-δ\deltaδ proof shows what the formal definition checks. To prove lim⁡x→3(2x−1)=5\lim_{x\to 3}(2x-1)=5limx→3​(2x−1)=5, start with the desired bound:

∣(2x−1)−5∣=∣2x−6∣=2∣x−3∣.|(2x-1)-5|=|2x-6|=2|x-3|.∣(2x−1)−5∣=∣2x−6∣=2∣x−3∣.

If we want this to be less than ϵ\epsilonϵ, it is enough to require ∣x−3∣<ϵ/2\vert x-3\vert \lt \epsilon/2∣x−3∣<ϵ/2. Therefore choose δ=ϵ/2\delta=\epsilon/2δ=ϵ/2. Then whenever 0<∣x−3∣<δ0\lt \vert x-3\vert \lt \delta0<∣x−3∣<δ,

∣(2x−1)−5∣=2∣x−3∣<2δ=2⋅ϵ2=ϵ.|(2x-1)-5|=2|x-3|<2\delta=2\cdot\frac{\epsilon}{2}=\epsilon.∣(2x−1)−5∣=2∣x−3∣<2δ=2⋅2ϵ​=ϵ.

The proof does not rely on a table or graph. It shows that every requested output tolerance ϵ\epsilonϵ can be met by a corresponding input tolerance δ\deltaδ. For more complicated functions, the algebra may require extra bounding steps, but the logic is the same: translate closeness in xxx into closeness in f(x)f(x)f(x).

Continuity also interacts well with composition. If ggg is continuous at aaa and fff is continuous at g(a)g(a)g(a), then f∘gf\circ gf∘g is continuous at aaa. This is why many everyday limits are solved by substitution: polynomials, rational functions away from zero denominators, roots on their domains, trigonometric functions, exponentials, and logarithms are continuous where defined. When a composed expression fails, the failure usually comes from a domain restriction, a denominator becoming zero, or a piecewise change in formula.

That diagnostic habit keeps computation tied to the graph instead of turning limits into memorized algebra or isolated symbol pushing.

Worked example 1: removable discontinuity​

Problem. Compute

lim⁡x→2x2−4x−2\lim_{x\to 2}\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}x→2lim​x−2x2−4​

and explain whether the original function is continuous at x=2x=2x=2.

Method.

  1. Direct substitution gives
22−42−2=00,\frac{2^2-4}{2-2}=\frac00,2−222−4​=00​,

so the expression is indeterminate.

  1. Factor the numerator:
x2−4=(x−2)(x+2).x^2-4=(x-2)(x+2).x2−4=(x−2)(x+2).
  1. Cancel only for x≠2x\ne 2x=2:
x2−4x−2=(x−2)(x+2)x−2=x+2.\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}=\frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2}=x+2.x−2x2−4​=x−2(x−2)(x+2)​=x+2.
  1. Now take the limit:
lim⁡x→2(x+2)=4.\lim_{x\to 2}(x+2)=4.x→2lim​(x+2)=4.

Checked answer. The limit is 444. The original function is not continuous at x=2x=2x=2 because it is not defined there. If we define f(2)=4f(2)=4f(2)=4, the discontinuity becomes removable.

Worked example 2: one-sided limits and a jump​

Problem. Let

f(x)={x+1,x<1,3,x≥1.f(x)= \begin{cases} x+1, & x<1,\\ 3, & x\ge 1. \end{cases}f(x)={x+1,3,​x<1,x≥1.​

Method.

  1. Approach from the left using the rule for x<1x\lt 1x<1:
lim⁡x→1−f(x)=lim⁡x→1−(x+1)=2.\lim_{x\to 1^-}f(x)=\lim_{x\to 1^-}(x+1)=2.x→1−lim​f(x)=x→1−lim​(x+1)=2.
  1. Approach from the right using the rule for x≥1x\ge 1x≥1:
lim⁡x→1+f(x)=lim⁡x→1+3=3.\lim_{x\to 1^+}f(x)=\lim_{x\to 1^+}3=3.x→1+lim​f(x)=x→1+lim​3=3.
  1. Compare one-sided limits:
2≠3.2\ne 3.2=3.
  1. Evaluate the function:
f(1)=3,f(1)=3,f(1)=3,

def sample_limit_values(f, a, steps=(1e-1, 1e-2, 1e-3, 1e-4)):
    for h in steps:

        right = f(a + h)
        print(f"h={h:g}: left={left:.8f}, right={right:.8f}")

def removable(x):
    return (x * x - 4) / (x - 2)

Common pitfalls​

  • Substituting into an indeterminate form and concluding that no limit exists. The form 0/00/00/0 means more work is needed.
  • Saying an infinite limit "exists" as a real number. Infinite limits describe unbounded behavior.
  • Forgetting to compare left and right limits for piecewise functions, absolute values, and rational functions with sign changes.
  • Canceling a factor and then forgetting that the original expression may still be undefined at the canceled value.
  • Confusing continuity at a point with continuity on an interval. Endpoints use one-sided continuity.
  • Treating numerical tables as proof. Tables suggest behavior, but algebra or theorems justify the answer.
