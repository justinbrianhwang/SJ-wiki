Parametric Polar and Conic Curves

Parametric, polar, and conic descriptions give alternatives to writing a curve as y=f(x)y=f(x)y=f(x). Parametric equations use a third variable, often time, to describe motion along a curve. Polar coordinates describe points by distance and angle. Conics describe curves such as parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas through geometric focus-directrix or algebraic relationships.

These descriptions are useful because many curves fail the vertical line test, loop back on themselves, or are naturally described by rotation. Calculus still applies, but derivatives, area, and arc length must be translated into the coordinate system being used.

Figure: Cardioid curve r=a(1+cos⁡θ)r=a(1+\cos\theta)r=a(1+cosθ). Image: Wikimedia Commons, D.328, CC BY-SA 2.1 JP.

Definitions​

A parametrized plane curve is given by

x=f(t),y=g(t),x=f(t),\qquad y=g(t),x=f(t),y=g(t),

where ttt lies in an interval. The point on the curve is

r(t)=⟨f(t),g(t)⟩.\mathbf{r}(t)=\langle f(t),g(t)\rangle.r(t)=⟨f(t),g(t)⟩.

If dx/dt≠0dx/dt\ne 0dx/dt=0, then the slope of the tangent line is

dydx=dy/dtdx/dt.\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}.dxdy​=dx/dtdy/dt​.

The second derivative is

d2ydx2=ddt(dydx)/dxdt.\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} =\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)\bigg/\frac{dx}{dt}.dx2d2y​=dtd​(dxdy​)/dtdx​.

Polar coordinates represent a point by

x=rcos⁡θ,y=rsin⁡θ.x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta.x=rcosθ,y=rsinθ.

A polar curve has form r=f(θ)r=f(\theta)r=f(θ). Its area over α≤θ≤β\alpha\le\theta\le\betaα≤θ≤β is

A=12∫αβr2 dθ.A=\frac12\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} r^2\,d\theta.A=21​∫αβ​r2dθ.

Conic sections can be defined as sets of points whose distances from a focus and a directrix have constant ratio eee, the eccentricity. If e=1e=1e=1, the conic is a parabola. If 0<e<10\lt e\lt 10<e<1, it is an ellipse. If e>1e\gt 1e>1, it is a hyperbola.

Key results​

For parametric curves, the tangent line at t=t0t=t_0t=t0​ uses the point

(x0,y0)=(f(t0),g(t0))(x_0,y_0)=(f(t_0),g(t_0))(x0​,y0​)=(f(t0​),g(t0​))

and slope

m=g′(t0)f′(t0)m=\frac{g'(t_0)}{f'(t_0)}m=f′(t0​)g′(t0​)​

when f′(t0)≠0f'(t_0)\ne 0f′(t0​)=0. If f′(t0)=0f'(t_0)=0f′(t0​)=0 and g′(t0)≠0g'(t_0)\ne 0g′(t0​)=0, the tangent may be vertical.

Parametric arc length is

L=∫ab(dxdt)2+(dydt)2 dt.L=\int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt.L=∫ab​(dtdx​)2+(dtdy​)2​dt.

This is the integral of speed along the curve.

For polar curves, tangent slope can be found by treating xxx and yyy as parametric functions of θ\thetaθ:

x=rcos⁡θ,y=rsin⁡θ.x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta.x=rcosθ,y=rsinθ.

Then

dydx=dydθdxdθ=r′sin⁡θ+rcos⁡θr′cos⁡θ−rsin⁡θ.\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\frac{dy}{d\theta}}{\frac{dx}{d\theta}} = \frac{r' \sin\theta+r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta-r\sin\theta}.dxdy​=dθdx​dθdy​​=r′cosθ−rsinθr′sinθ+rcosθ​.

Polar area comes from sectors. A small sector with radius rrr and angle dθd\thetadθ has approximate area

dA=12r2 dθ,dA=\frac12 r^2\,d\theta,dA=21​r2dθ,

which leads to the integral formula.

Standard conic equations centered at the origin include:

x2a2+y2b2=1\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1a2x2​+b2y2​=1

for an ellipse, and

x2a2−y2b2=1\frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1a2x2​−b2y2​=1

for a horizontal hyperbola. A parabola with vertex at the origin and focus (p,0)(p,0)(p,0) has equation

y2=4px.y^2=4px.y2=4px.

In polar form, a conic with focus at the pole can often be written

r=ed1±ecos⁡θorr=ed1±esin⁡θ,r=\frac{ed}{1\pm e\cos\theta} \quad\text{or}\quad r=\frac{ed}{1\pm e\sin\theta},r=1±ecosθed​orr=1±esinθed​,

depending on the directrix orientation.

The slope formula for parametric curves follows directly from the chain rule. If yyy is viewed as a function of xxx along the curve and x=x(t)x=x(t)x=x(t), then

dydt=dydxdxdt.\frac{dy}{dt}=\frac{dy}{dx}\frac{dx}{dt}.dtdy​=dxdy​dtdx​.

When dx/dt≠0dx/dt\ne 0dx/dt=0, solving gives

dydx=dy/dtdx/dt.\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}.dxdy​=dx/dtdy/dt​.

This also explains the vertical tangent case: if dx/dt=0dx/dt=0dx/dt=0 while dy/dt≠0dy/dt\ne 0dy/dt=0, the curve is moving vertically in the plane.

Parametric equations can trace the same geometric curve more than once. The interval for ttt is therefore part of the curve description. For example, x=cos⁡tx=\cos tx=cost, y=sin⁡ty=\sin ty=sint traces the unit circle once on 0≤t≤2π0\le t\le 2\pi0≤t≤2π, twice on 0≤t≤4π0\le t\le 4\pi0≤t≤4π, and only an arc on a shorter interval. When computing arc length or area, overtracing changes the accumulated value.

Polar symmetry can reduce work. If replacing θ\thetaθ by −θ-\theta−θ gives the same equation, the curve is symmetric about the polar axis. If replacing θ\thetaθ by π−θ\pi-\thetaπ−θ gives the same equation, it is symmetric about the vertical axis. If replacing rrr by −r-r−r gives the same equation, there is symmetry about the pole. Symmetry is helpful, but it should be verified against the actual tracing interval.

Conics connect algebra and geometry. Completing the square can reveal a shifted center or vertex. Eccentricity describes shape independent of scale: ellipses have eccentricity less than 111, circles have e=0e=0e=0, parabolas have e=1e=1e=1, and hyperbolas have eccentricity greater than 111. In orbital models, this eccentricity measures how far an orbit deviates from circular.

Arc length formulas also depend on the coordinate system. For a polar curve,

L=∫αβr2+(drdθ)2 dθ.L=\int_{\alpha}^{\beta}\sqrt{r^2+\left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2}\,d\theta.L=∫αβ​r2+(dθdr​)2​dθ.

This comes from differentiating x=rcos⁡θx=r\cos\thetax=rcosθ and y=rsin⁡θy=r\sin\thetay=rsinθ, then using the parametric arc length formula with parameter θ\thetaθ.

Area for parametric curves can be computed from

A=∫y dx=∫αβy(t)x′(t) dtA=\int y\,dx =\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} y(t)x'(t)\,dtA=∫ydx=∫αβ​y(t)x′(t)dt

when the orientation and region are appropriate. If the curve is closed, Green's Theorem later gives symmetric formulas such as

A=12∫(x dy−y dx).A=\frac12\int (x\,dy-y\,dx).A=21​∫(xdy−ydx).

Even in single-variable calculus, the key is that dxdxdx becomes x′(t) dtx'(t)\,dtx′(t)dt.

For conics, completing the square is the main algebraic skill. An equation such as

x2+4x+4y2−8y=8x^2+4x+4y^2-8y=8x2+4x+4y2−8y=8

should be rewritten as

(x+2)2+4(y−1)2=16(x+2)^2+4(y-1)^2=16(x+2)2+4(y−1)2=16

before classification. The center is (−2,1)(-2,1)(−2,1), and the different coefficients reveal the semi-axis lengths. Without completing the square, shifted conics are easy to misread.

Polar equations can also represent the same point in multiple ways:

(r,θ)=(−r,θ+π).(r,\theta)=(-r,\theta+\pi).(r,θ)=(−r,θ+π).

This nonuniqueness is useful but can create tracing errors. A table of key angles, zeros, and maximum radii often prevents double-counting.

Parameter orientation also affects signed quantities. If a curve is traversed in the opposite direction, a signed area integral such as ∫y dx\int y\,dx∫ydx may change sign, even though the geometric area is unchanged. Arc length is different because speed is nonnegative, so reversing the parameter interval does not change total length when handled with the correct bounds.

For conics, focus and directrix definitions are not just historical. They explain reflective properties: rays parallel to a parabola's axis reflect through the focus, and ellipses reflect rays from one focus to the other. These geometric properties are why conics appear in optics, satellite dishes, planetary motion, and whispering galleries.

Worked example 1: tangent and concavity for a parametric curve​

Problem. For

x=t2+1,y=t3−t,x=t^2+1,\qquad y=t^3-t,x=t2+1,y=t3−t,

find dy/dxdy/dxdy/dx and the tangent line at t=1t=1t=1.

Method.

  1. Differentiate both coordinate functions:
dxdt=2t,dydt=3t2−1.\frac{dx}{dt}=2t, \qquad \frac{dy}{dt}=3t^2-1.dtdx​=2t,dtdy​=3t2−1.
  1. Form the parametric slope:
dydx=3t2−12t.\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{3t^2-1}{2t}.dxdy​=2t3t2−1​.
  1. Evaluate the point at t=1t=1t=1:
x(1)=12+1=2,y(1)=13−1=0.x(1)=1^2+1=2, \qquad y(1)=1^3-1=0.x(1)=12+1=2,y(1)=13−1=0.
  1. Evaluate the slope:
m=3(1)2−12(1)=22=1.m=\frac{3(1)^2-1}{2(1)}=\frac{2}{2}=1.m=2(1)3(1)2−1​=22​=1.
  1. Use point-slope form:
y−0=1(x−2).y-0=1(x-2).y−0=1(x−2).

Checked answer. The tangent line is y=x−2y=x-2y=x−2. Since dx/dt=2dx/dt=2dx/dt=2 at t=1t=1t=1, the slope formula is valid there.

If we also want concavity at t=1t=1t=1, differentiate the slope with respect to ttt:

dydx=3t2−12t=32t−12t.\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{3t^2-1}{2t}=\frac32t-\frac{1}{2t}.dxdy​=2t3t2−1​=23​t−2t1​.

Then

ddt(dydx)=32+12t2.\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)=\frac32+\frac{1}{2t^2}.dtd​(dxdy​)=23​+2t21​.

Now divide by dx/dt=2tdx/dt=2tdx/dt=2t:

d2ydx2=(32+12t2)12t.\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} =\left(\frac32+\frac{1}{2t^2}\right)\frac{1}{2t}.dx2d2y​=(23​+2t21​)2t1​.

At t=1t=1t=1, this equals

(32+12)12=1.\left(\frac32+\frac12\right)\frac12=1.(23​+21​)21​=1.

The curve is concave up at the tangent point.

Worked example 2: polar area​

Problem. Find the area inside one petal of

r=2sin⁡(3θ).r=2\sin(3\theta).r=2sin(3θ).

Method.

  1. One petal is traced while r≥0r\ge 0r≥0 between consecutive zeros:
2sin⁡(3θ)=0⇒3θ=0,π⇒0≤θ≤π3.2\sin(3\theta)=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 3\theta=0,\pi \quad\Rightarrow\quad 0\le\theta\le\frac{\pi}{3}.2sin(3θ)=0⇒3θ=0,π⇒0≤θ≤3π​.

A=12∫0π/3r2 dθ.A=\frac12\int_0^{\pi/3} r^2\,d\theta.A=21​∫0π/3​r2dθ.

A=12∫0π/34sin⁡2(3θ) dθ=2∫0π/3sin⁡2(3θ) dθ.A=\frac12\int_0^{\pi/3}4\sin^2(3\theta)\,d\theta =2\int_0^{\pi/3}\sin^2(3\theta)\,d\theta.A=21​∫0π/3​4sin2(3θ)dθ=2∫0π/3​sin2(3θ)dθ.
  1. Use
sin⁡2u=1−cos⁡(2u)2.\sin^2 u=\frac{1-\cos(2u)}{2}.sin2u=21−cos(2u)​.

Then

A=2∫0π/31−cos⁡(6θ)2 dθ=∫0π/3(1−cos⁡(6θ)) dθ.A=2\int_0^{\pi/3}\frac{1-\cos(6\theta)}{2}\,d\theta =\int_0^{\pi/3}(1-\cos(6\theta))\,d\theta.A=2∫0π/3​21−cos(6θ)​dθ=∫0π/3​(1−cos(6θ))dθ.
  1. Integrate:
A=[θ−sin⁡(6θ)6]0π/3.A=\left[\theta-\frac{\sin(6\theta)}{6}\right]_0^{\pi/3}.A=[θ−6sin(6θ)​]0π/3​.
  1. Evaluate:
A=π3−sin⁡(2π)6−0=π3.A=\frac{\pi}{3}-\frac{\sin(2\pi)}{6}-0=\frac{\pi}{3}.A=3π​−6sin(2π)​−0=3π​.

Checked answer. One petal has area π/3\pi/3π/3. The full curve has three petals, so the total area is π\piπ.

The bounds are the most important part of this example. Integrating from 000 to 2π2\pi2π would count the three-petal rose more than once for some rose equations. A reliable approach is to find consecutive zeros of rrr that enclose one traced loop and then use symmetry only after confirming how many distinct loops occur.

The maximum radius of this petal occurs when sin⁡(3θ)=1\sin(3\theta)=1sin(3θ)=1, so 3θ=π/23\theta=\pi/23θ=π/2 and θ=π/6\theta=\pi/6θ=π/6. At that angle, r=2r=2r=2, matching the midpoint of the interval [0,π/3][0,\pi/3][0,π/3].

Checking a midpoint angle is a quick way to confirm that the selected interval traces a full petal rather than only half of one.

Code​

from math import sin, cos, pi

def polar_to_cartesian(r, theta):
    return r * cos(theta), r * sin(theta)

def rose(theta):
    return 2 * sin(3 * theta)

for k in range(4):
    theta = k * pi / 9
    print(theta, polar_to_cartesian(rose(theta), theta))

Common pitfalls​

  • Using dy/dtdy/dtdy/dt as the slope of a parametric curve. The Cartesian slope is (dy/dt)/(dx/dt)(dy/dt)/(dx/dt)(dy/dt)/(dx/dt).
  • Dividing by dx/dtdx/dtdx/dt when dx/dt=0dx/dt=0dx/dt=0 without checking for a vertical tangent.
  • Forgetting the factor 1/21/21/2 in polar area.
  • Integrating a polar curve over too large an interval and counting petals or loops more than once.
  • Treating negative rrr values as impossible. In polar coordinates, negative rrr plots in the opposite direction.
  • Classifying conics from equations without completing the square when the center is shifted.
